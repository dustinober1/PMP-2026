# 4.1a Stakeholder Mapping

**ECO Task**: Engage stakeholders

Identifying who your stakeholders are is the first step, but **mapping** them tells you where to spend your limited energy and political capital. Effective leaders use these models to prioritize their attention.

## ‚úÖ How to Place Stakeholders (Fast and Defensible)

On the PMP exam, "power" is not just job title‚Äîit is the ability to **influence decisions, resources, or acceptance**.

### Power Assessment Criteria

**Formal Power Signals:**
- Controls budget allocation or approval authority
- Can reassign people or change priorities
- Has hiring/firing influence over team members
- Owns governance gates or sign-off authority

**Informal Power Signals:**
- Has the sponsor's ear or executive relationships
- Controls critical technical knowledge or expertise
- Shapes organizational culture or opinion
- Has historical influence ("they've been here forever")

### Interest Assessment Criteria

**Direct Impact Indicators:**
- The project changes their KPIs, metrics, or performance reviews
- Their workflow, tools, or processes will change
- They have direct benefit or downside risk from the outcome
- They are accountable for outcomes the project impacts

**Indirect Impact Indicators:**
- Their team or department will be affected
- Budget or resources they control are involved
- Political capital or reputation at stake
- Future initiatives depend on this project's success

::: tip üîé Quick Calibration
If they can **stop** the project, treat them as **High Power**. If the project changes their day-to-day work or metrics, treat them as **High Interest**.
:::

### Dynamic Movement: Stakeholders Shift Quadrants

Stakeholder positions are not static. Common triggers for movement:

| Trigger | Typical Movement |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Promoted/Demoted** | Power changes; may move left/right |
| **Project scope affects them** | Interest increases; moves up |
| **Reorganization** | Power and interest may both shift |
| **Negative experience** | Interest increases (often negatively) |
| **Champion leaves organization** | May lose an ally in high-power quadrant |
| **Regulatory scrutiny** | External stakeholder power increases |

**Re-map when**: Sponsor/leadership changes, major scope change, re-orgs, vendor changes, regulatory scrutiny, or public/customer impact increases.

## üéØ The Salience Model

For complex projects with large ecosystems, simple grids aren't enough. The **Salience Model** analyzes three overlapping attributes:

1. **Power**: Their level of formal or informal authority.
2. **Legitimacy**: Their legal or moral right to influence the outcome.
3. **Urgency**: How quickly they require your attention.

## üßä The Stakeholder Cube

The Cube adds a critical third dimension to the Power/Interest Grid: **Attitude**.

### Three Attitude States

- **Supportive (+)**: Actively wants the project to succeed
- **Neutral (0)**: Neither supporting nor opposing; waiting to see
- **Resistant (-)**: Actively or passively opposing the project

### Stakeholder Cube Strategies

| Power | Interest | Attitude | Strategy |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| High | High | Supportive | Champion: Empower as advocate, involve in decisions |
| High | High | Neutral | Convert: Demonstrate value, build relationship, invite input |
| High | High | Resistant | Priority Risk: Direct engagement, root cause discovery, sponsor involvement |
| High | Low | Supportive | Ally: Maintain with periodic executive updates |
| High | Low | Neutral | Monitor: Keep satisfied, prevent negative shifts |
| High | Low | Resistant | Danger Zone: Immediate attention, remove blockers |
| Low | High | Supportive | Advocate: Leverage enthusiasm, involve in testing/feedback |
| Low | High | Neutral | Inform: Regular updates to build connection |
| Low | High | Resistant | Contain: Monitor, limit influence, clarify facts |
| Low | Low | Any | Minimal: Broadcast comms only, watch for changes |

::: info üß† Using the Cube on the Exam
If a stakeholder is **High Power** and **Resistant**, the best answers usually involve **direct engagement**, **root-cause discovery**, and an **updated engagement plan** (not avoidance or bypassing).
:::

## üìç Influence Direction Model

Understanding how stakeholders are influenced‚Äîand who influences them‚Äîhelps you design effective engagement chains.

### Influence Mapping Questions

1. **Who do they listen to?** (Their influencers)
2. **Who listens to them?** (Their influence reach)
3. **What sources do they trust?** (Data, experts, peers)
4. **How do they form opinions?** (Evidence, relationships, authority)

### Coalition Building Using Influence Maps

| Scenario | Influence Strategy |
| :--- | :--- |
| Need executive buy-in | Identify who the executive trusts; engage them first |
| Technical skeptic blocking | Find respected technical peer to advocate |
| Cultural resistance | Identify early adopters to create social proof |
| External stakeholder concern | Use sponsor or governance to provide formal backing |

## üîÑ When to Re-Map Stakeholders

Stakeholder mapping is a living activity. Re-run your analysis when:

- **Organizational changes**: Mergers, re-orgs, leadership changes
- **Project changes**: Major scope additions, timeline shifts, budget adjustments
- **Environmental changes**: Regulatory shifts, market changes, competitive moves
- **Relationship changes**: Trust broken, new relationships formed
- **Phase transitions**: Moving from planning to execution, execution to closure
- **Conflict emergence**: Any significant stakeholder dispute
