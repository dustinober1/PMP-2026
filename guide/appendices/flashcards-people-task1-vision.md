## Project Charter Fundamentals

### Card 1
**Front**: What is the Project Charter?
**Back**: The "Birth Certificate" of the project—the formal document that authorizes the project to exist and allows the PM to use company resources.
**Domain**: People | **Task**: Develop a common vision

---

### Card 2
**Front**: Why is the Project Charter the PM's source of power?
**Back**: Without an approved charter, you are just a person with an idea, not a Project Manager. The charter proves you are authorized by Senior Management to lead the project and request resources.
**Domain**: People | **Task**: Develop a common vision

---

### Card 3
**Front**: Who signs off on (authorizes) the Project Charter?
**Back**: The Sponsor—the person providing the money and resources for the project.
**Domain**: People | **Task**: Develop a common vision

---

### Card 4
**Front**: What does "PM Appointment" in a charter mean?
**Back**: The charter explicitly names the Project Manager and defines their level of authority (e.g., "approve expenditures up to $10,000 without sponsor approval").
**Domain**: People | **Task**: Develop a common vision

---

### Card 5
**Front**: What is the "Strategic Anchor" element of a charter?
**Back**: The high-level goals, success criteria, and summary milestones that connect the project to organizational strategy.
**Domain**: People | **Task**: Develop a common vision

---

### Card 6
**Front**: What does "Resource Commitment" in a charter represent?
**Back**: The budget envelope and organizational commitment to provide the resources needed for the project.
**Domain**: People | **Task**: Develop a common vision

---

### Card 7
**Front**: In the project lifecycle, where does the Charter fit?
**Back**: Business Need → Needs Assessment → Business Case → PROJECT CHARTER → Project Planning. The Charter is the "authorization gate" where work legally begins.
**Domain**: People | **Task**: Develop a common vision

---

### Card 8
**Front**: If asked to begin project execution without an approved charter, what should you do?
**Back**: Work with the sponsor to finalize and approve the charter first. Never start execution (hire vendors, build deliverables, spend budget) without authorization.
**Domain**: People | **Task**: Develop a common vision

## Charter vs. Other Documents

### Card 33
**Front**: What is the difference between a Project Charter and Project Management Plan?
**Back**: Charter = Authorization + high-level guardrails. PM Plan = Execution strategy + baselines. The Charter is NOT a detailed plan.
**Domain**: People | **Task**: Develop a common vision

---

### Card 34
**Front**: What is the difference between a Project Charter and Business Case?
**Back**: Charter = Authorization and PM assignment. Business Case = Investment justification (value). The Business Case does NOT assign PM authority.
**Domain**: People | **Task**: Develop a common vision

### Card 35
**Front**: What is the difference between a Project Charter and Contract/SOW?
**Back**: Charter = Internal governance and PM authority. Contract/SOW = External deliverables and legal obligations.
**Domain**: People | **Task**: Develop a common vision

## Team Charter Fundamentals

### Card 47
**Front**: What is a Team Charter?
**Back**: A living document created BY the team, FOR the team. It sets the guardrails for high-trust culture and defines how the team will work together.
**Domain**: People | **Task**: Develop a common vision

---

### Card 48
**Front**: What are the 4 main elements of a Team Charter?
**Back**: 1) Team Values (what we stand for), 2) Ground Rules (behavioral "laws"), 3) Communication norms (tools and response times), 4) Decision Making (how we decide).
**Domain**: People | **Task**: Develop a common vision

---

### Card 49
**Front**: What are Ground Rules in a Team Charter?
**Back**: Behavioral "laws" that govern team behavior. Examples: "Always have cameras on," "No phones during standups," "Assume positive intent."
**Domain**: People | **Task**: Develop a common vision

---

### Card 50
**Front**: What 3 principles make Ground Rules effective?
**Back**: 1) Collaborative (team must write them to own them), 2) Visible (displayed prominently), 3) Self-Policing (team calls out violations without PM playing "police").
**Domain**: People | **Task**: Develop a common vision

---

### Card 51

## Definition of Done vs. Acceptance Criteria

### Card 52
**Front**: What is the difference between Definition of Done (DoD) and Acceptance Criteria (AC)?
**Back**: DoD = applies to ALL items, created by team once, quality gate for everything. AC = applies to ONE specific item, created by Product Owner per story, specific requirements for one deliverable.
**Domain**: People | **Task**: Develop a common vision

## Decision Models

### Card 56
**Front**: When should you use Consensus decision-making?
**Back**: For high-impact decisions that require buy-in. Everyone agrees (or consents). Risk: Slow if not time-boxed; may water down bold ideas.
**Domain**: People | **Task**: Develop a common vision

---

### Card 57
**Front**: When should you use Majority Vote decision-making?
**Back**: For time-boxed decisions with moderate impact. 50%+1 wins. Risk: Minority may disengage; doesn't resolve root disagreement.
**Domain**: People | **Task**: Develop a common vision

---

### Card 58
**Front**: When should you use Expert/Delegated decision-making?
**Back**: For specialized technical decisions. One trusted person decides. Risk: Others may feel excluded; expert may have blind spots.
**Domain**: People | **Task**: Develop a common vision

---

### Card 59
**Front**: What is the Consent Model (Sociocracy) for decision-making?
**Back**: Ask: "Do you have a principled objection to this proposal?" If no one has a substantive blocker, the proposal passes. Faster than consensus but still collaborative.
**Domain**: People | **Task**: Develop a common vision

---

### Card 60
**Front**: What should a Decision Log include?
**Back**: Decision ID, Date, Decision, Context, Alternatives Considered, Decider, Status.
**Domain**: People | **Task**: Develop a common vision

## Conflict and Escalation

### Card 61
**Front**: What are the 4 steps in an Escalation Ladder?
**Back**: 1) Self-resolution (parties address directly), 2) Facilitated session (PM/SM helps), 3) Wider alignment (involve functional managers), 4) Escalate (sponsor decides—last resort).
**Domain**: People | **Task**: Develop a common vision

---

### Card 62
**Front**: When SHOULD you escalate a conflict?
**Back**: When: decision is outside team authority, conflict impacts project viability, resource constraint can't be negotiated, or safety/compliance is at risk.
**Domain**: People | **Task**: Develop a common vision

---

### Card 63
**Front**: When should you NOT escalate a conflict?
**Back**: When: team can resolve with facilitation, conflict is healthy technical debate, negotiation hasn't been tried, or issue is preference not principle.
**Domain**: People | **Task**: Develop a common vision

---

### Card 64
**Front**: What is the "24-Hour Rule" for conflicts?
**Back**: If a conflict isn't resolved within 24 hours of surfacing, escalate to the next level. This prevents issues from festering.
**Domain**: People | **Task**: Develop a common vision
