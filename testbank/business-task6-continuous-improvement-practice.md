### Question 1

**Enabler:** 1 | **Methodology:** Predictive

**Scenario:**
A public-sector agency is restarting a data warehouse modernization effort after a similar project failed last year due to late defect discovery and unclear acceptance criteria. The sponsor wants to “move fast” by reusing the old schedule and starting build work immediately. Several team members from the prior project have moved on, and the remaining team only has informal memories of what went wrong.

**Question:**
What is the MOST IMPORTANT action for the project manager to take?

- **Correct:** Review archived lessons learned and translate them into updated acceptance criteria and quality checklists.
- **Distractor 1:** Reuse last year’s schedule baseline and add an extra testing phase near the end of the project.
- **Distractor 2:** Ask the sponsor to approve additional quality budget now to cover potential rework discovered during validation.
- **Distractor 3:** Start development immediately and capture improvement ideas later during the closing meeting to save time.

<details>
<summary>Detailed Remediation</summary>

| Aspect | Details |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Core Logic** | Continuous improvement starts by reusing organizational knowledge. Pulling lessons learned forward into acceptance criteria and checklists builds quality in, instead of repeating late-cycle defect discovery. |
| **PMI Mindset** | Treat lessons learned as reusable organizational assets, not tribal knowledge. Prevention and clarity outperform “test more later.” |
| **The Trap** | Adding late testing feels safer, but it accepts recurring defects and shifts quality work to the most expensive point in the lifecycle. |
| **Source Link** | `guide/09-monitoring/project-closure.md` |

</details>

## Key Takeaways: Continuous Improvement Pro-Tips

### 1. Quality Is Both Conformance and Fitness for Use
Passing tests is not enough if users cannot use the deliverable effectively. Treat feedback loops as quality data and refine requirements and acceptance criteria accordingly.

### 2. Build Quality In, Do Not Test Quality In
Prevention activities (training, reviews, automation, clear criteria) are cheaper and more effective than late-cycle inspection and rework. When defects repeat, fix the process cause.

### 3. Know When the Data Signals Action
Control limits and specification limits are not the same. A stable process can still be incapable. Patterns like seven consecutive points on one side of the mean require investigation, even without limit breaches.

### 4. Lessons Learned Must Be Reused, Not Just Recorded
A lessons learned register is only valuable when it changes how the next phase or project is run. Apply recommendations early, assign owners, and make improvement work visible.

### 5. Closure Completes Value Transfer
The project is not “done” at delivery alone. Formal acceptance, transition readiness, procurement closure, and organizational record archiving are required so the organization can operate and reuse what you learned.

### 6. OPAs Are the Enterprise Memory
Store lessons learned, templates, checklists, procurement evaluations, and audit evidence in a searchable, governed repository. This is how continuous improvement becomes organizational, not personal.
