### Question 1

**Enabler:** 1 | **Methodology:** Predictive

**Scenario:**
A pharmaceutical company is running a predictive clinical trial project. During the monitoring phase, the European Union passes a new regulation requiring additional safety reporting for all drug trials. The regulation takes effect in 90 days. Your project is currently on schedule to deliver in 120 days. The project sponsor is on a three-week vacation and unreachable.

**Question:**
What is the FIRST action for the project manager to take?

- **Correct:** Document the regulation in the risk register, assess the impact on scope, schedule, and cost, and prepare an impact analysis for the change control board
- **Distractor 1:** Wait until the sponsor returns from vacation to discuss the new regulation
- **Distractor 2:** Immediately halt all project work until the new requirements are fully understood
- **Distractor 3:** Contact the regulatory affairs team to request an exemption for your project

<details>
<summary>Detailed Remediation</summary>

| Aspect | Details |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Core Logic** | External regulatory changes require immediate impact assessment and documentation, even if key stakeholders are unavailable. The risk register is the right place to track new external risks, and the change control board can make decisions within approved tolerances. |
| **PMI Mindset** | Proactive monitoring and early response to external changes. The PM doesn't wait for stakeholders but takes initiative to assess and document impacts while following governance processes. |
| **The Trap** | Waiting for the sponsor seems respectful, but regulations have hard deadlines and the PM must act immediately. Halting work is premature without understanding the actual impact. |
| **Source Link** | /guide/02-strategic/external-environment.md - Section on Early Warning Signals and Impact Assessment Process |

</details>

## Key Takeaways: External Environment Pro-Tips

### 1. The 5-Step Response Model Is Your Best Friend
When external changes hit, follow this sequence: **Identify → Evaluate → Escalate → Respond → Update**. Don't skip the analysis phase, and don't make unilateral decisions if the change affects the business case. Document everything.

### 2. Early Warning Signals Beat Crisis Response
Set up a **scanning cadence** (weekly for industry news, monthly for regulatory calendars, quarterly for competitor/supplier analysis). The "watch list" (proposed regulations, emerging tech, economic trends) gives you time to prepare rather than panic.

### 3. Know Your Alert Triggers
- **Immediate (Red):** Stop-work triggers (new laws making work illegal, safety incidents)
- **Urgent (Orange):** Near-term impact (regulations with 90-day deadlines, competitor launches)
- **Watch (Yellow):** Developing situations (proposed laws, economic trends)
- **Informational (Green):** Future consideration (long-term forecasts, emerging tech)

### 4. Compliance Is Non-Negotiable, But How You Comply Is Flexible
When new regulations arrive, you can't ignore them—but you have three response paths: **Absorb** (modify plan within current governance), **Pivot** (change project goals/deliverables), or **Cancel** (recommend closure if no longer viable). **Governance decides which path, not the PM.**

### 5. External Changes That Hit the Business Case Require Escalation
If a change affects scope/schedule/cost within your approved tolerance, you can manage it through the change control process. If it affects the **business case** or **value proposition**, escalate to governance for a decision.

### 6. Don't Confuse "Assess" with "Ignore"
Common trap: "It's just a proposed regulation, so we'll wait until it's law." Better approach: Add it to the risk register, monitor progress, and have a contingency plan ready. Early assessment beats rushed decisions.
