### Question 1

**Enabler:** 2. Recommend a project management development approach | **Methodology:** Predictive

**Scenario:**
A project manager is assigned to a high-rise construction project in a metropolitan area with strict building codes and environmental regulations. The stakeholders require a fixed-price contract and a clear understanding of the final results before any construction begins. The technical requirements for the foundation and structural integrity are well-documented and have been used in similar previous projects.

**Question:**
According to the Stacey Matrix logic in the local ways-of-working guide, which development approach should the project manager recommend for this initiative?

- **Correct:** A Predictive approach because the requirements and technical certainty are both high, making it a "Simple" or "Linear" project where following a pre-defined plan is most efficient.
- **Distractor 1:** An Agile approach to allow for frequent feedback loops from city inspectors as the construction progresses through different levels of the high-rise.
- **Distractor 2:** A Hybrid approach where the design phase is predictive but the actual construction phase uses Scrum to manage daily tasks and impediments.
- **Distractor 3:** A Chaotic approach to address the immediate regulatory hurdles and environmental risks that might appear during the excavation phase.

<details>
<summary>Detailed Remediation</summary>

| Aspect | Details |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Core Logic** | The Stacey Matrix classifies projects with high requirement and technical certainty as 'Simple' or 'Linear'. Predictive (Waterfall) is best suited here because rework is expensive and the path is well-understood. |
| **PMI Mindset** | Assess the stability of requirements and the technical complexity before selecting the methodology. Stability and regulatory constraints favor predictive. |
| **The Trap** | The "city inspectors" distractor is tempting because it mentions feedback, but in heavy construction, feedback is usually for compliance validation (Phase Gates), not for evolving the scope. |
| **Source Link** | `guide/01-introduction/ways-of-working.md` |

</details>

### Question 2

**Scenario:**
A global organization is launching a new software platform across multiple regions. The project manager has decided to use a predictive approach for the regional infrastructure setup and regulatory compliance, while the training content and user-facing features will be developed using two-week sprints. **Question:**
What is the best action for the project manager to take to integrate these two approaches into a cohesive plan?

- **Distractor 1:** Require the Agile teams to convert their story points into a Gantt chart format to ensure full visibility within the predictive master plan.
- **Distractor 2:** Transition the entire project to a predictive methodology to ensure that the infrastructure milestones are not jeopardized by the uncertainty of the software sprints.
- **Distractor 3:** Eliminate the phase-gate reviews for the infrastructure components to match the continuous delivery speed of the Agile software teams.

<details>
<summary>Detailed Remediation</summary>

| Aspect | Details |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Core Logic** | A hybrid model often uses predictive "guardrails" (milestones/gates) for fixed elements like infrastructure, while allowing teams to remain agile for evolving content. |
| **PMI Mindset** | Integration in a hybrid environment involves balancing the need for corporate governance (milestones) with team-level flexibility (sprints). |
| **The Trap** | Converting story points to Gantt charts is a common mistake that forces agile work into a rigid predictive container, losing the benefits of adaptive planning. |

### Question 3

**Enabler:** 6. Estimate work effort and resource requirements | **Methodology:** Agile

**Scenario:**
A newly formed Scrum team is in their first sprint planning session. They have a product backlog with several high-priority user stories. The Product Owner is asking for a definitive delivery date for the first release, but the team has no historical data regarding their performance.

**Question:**
What should the project manager (Scrum Master) advise the team and Product Owner to do regarding estimation?

- **Correct:** Use relative estimation techniques like story points for the backlog items and wait for several sprints to establish a stable velocity before providing a reliable forecast.
- **Distractor 1:** Calculate the exact number of hours for each task in the first sprint and multiply by the total number of items in the backlog to determine the release date.
- **Distractor 2:** Use a definitive estimation range of -5% to +10% based on the expert judgment of the senior developers to set the release date immediately.
- **Distractor 3:** Request the team to commit to a fixed velocity based on industry standards for similar software projects to provide the sponsor with a date.

<details>
<summary>Detailed Remediation</summary>

| Aspect | Details |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Core Logic** | Agile uses relative measures (story points) and empirical data (velocity) to forecast. Without historical data, the team must establish velocity first. |
| **PMI Mindset** | Avoid "false precision." In high-uncertainty environments, definitive estimates are not possible on day one; instead, use empirical measurement over time. |
| **The Trap** | Using "definitive estimation" is a predictive concept (-5% to +10%) and is inappropriate for early-stage agile projects where velocity hasn't been established. |
| **Source Link** | `guide/01-introduction/ways-of-working.md` |

</details>

| **PMI Mindset** | Emphasize the value of traceability in defending against missed scope and ensuring compliance with stakeholder needs. |
| **The Trap** | The RTM is NOT part of the Scope Baseline (which is Scope Statement + WBS + WBS Dictionary), though it is a critical supporting document. |

| **The Trap** | Submitting a change request to a PMO for an internal team agreement like the DoD is a predictive bottleneck that goes against agile principles of self-organization. |

| **PMI Mindset** | Tailor the agile framework to the pace of the work. If sprints become a bottleneck for response time, Kanban's pull-based system is a more efficient choice. |
| **The Trap** | 24-hour sprints would result in the team spending almost all their time in planning, reviews, and retrospectives rather than actually delivering value. |

## Key Takeaways: Process Task 1 Pro-Tips
1. **The Integration Glue:** In hybrid projects, Configuration and Change Management are the essential "glue" that keeps predictive hardware and agile software aligned.
2. **Methodology Agnosticism:** Use the Stacey Matrix (Requirements vs. Technical Certainty) to scientifically choose your approach. Don't let organizational trends dictate an ill-fitting methodology.
3. **The Hexagon Mindset:** The triple constraint (Scope, Time, Cost) is now a hexagon that includes Quality, Risk, and Resources. Every planning decision ripples across all six.
4. **Value Over Volume:** In Agile, track Burnup charts and "Value Delivered" metrics. High velocity is useless if the team is delivering features that provide no customer adoption or business benefit.
5. **Progressive Elaboration is the Law:** You don't need perfect info on Day 1. Use Planning Packages, Rolling Wave Planning, and Backlog Refinement to detail work as you learn more.
6. **Sustainability is a Requirement:** Integrate ESG and sustainability goals into the WBS Dictionary and the Definition of Done. If it's not in the planning artifacts, it won't be delivered.
