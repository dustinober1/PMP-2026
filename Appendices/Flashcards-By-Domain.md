## People Domain (33%)

### Leadership and Team Management

**Q**: What are the five stages of team development in Tuckman's model?
**Domain**: People | **Chapter**: 6a
**A**: Forming, Storming, Norming, Performing, Adjourning

---

**Q**: What characterizes the Forming stage of team development?
**Domain**: People | **Chapter**: 6a
**A**: Team members are getting to know each other, roles are unclear, people are polite and excited but uncertain, productivity is low

---

**Q**: What should a PM focus on during the Storming stage?
**Domain**: People | **Chapter**: 6a
**A**: Do not avoid conflict; lean into it, listen to concerns, facilitate resolution, help team see conflict is normal and necessary

---

**Q**: What are the key characteristics of a high-performing team in the Performing stage?
**Domain**: People | **Chapter**: 6a
**A**: Team is autonomous, high productivity and quality, conflicts resolved quickly within team, people trust each other, team takes pride in work

---

**Q**: What is the primary difference between servant leadership and traditional leadership?
**Domain**: People | **Chapter**: 6f, 9b
**A**: Servant leadership focuses on serving the team and removing obstacles, while traditional leadership focuses on directing and controlling team activities

---

**Q**: What are the three key motivations according to Daniel Pink's research?
**Domain**: People | **Chapter**: 6e
**A**: Autonomy (control over work), Mastery (getting better at skills), Purpose (work matters and has impact)

---

**Q**: What is psychological safety and why is it important?
**Domain**: People | **Chapter**: 6d
**A**: People believe they can speak up with ideas, questions, or concerns without being punished or humiliated. Essential for conflict resolution and team performance.

---

**Q**: What are the four leadership styles in Situational Leadership?
**Domain**: People | **Chapter**: 9a
**A**: Directive (high direction, low support), Coaching (high direction, high support), Participative (low direction, high support), Delegating (low direction, low support)

---

**Q**: When should you use a Directive leadership style?
**Domain**: People | **Chapter**: 9a
**A**: Crisis situations, with new/inexperienced people, urgent simple tasks, clear expert/novice gap, safety-critical situations

---

**Q**: What leadership style is best for a "Capable but Cautious" team member?
**Domain**: People | **Chapter**: 9a
**A**: Participative/Collaborative - less direction, more dialogue, continued support and recognition, involve them in decisions

---

### Stakeholder Engagement

**Q**: In a Power/Interest Grid, how should you manage stakeholders with high power and high interest?
**Domain**: People | **Chapter**: 5a, 7a
**A**: Manage Closely - Engage frequently, involve in decisions, keep satisfied

---

**Q**: What are the four quadrants of the Power/Interest Grid?
**Domain**: People | **Chapter**: 7a
**A**: Manage Closely (high power, high interest), Keep Satisfied (high power, low interest), Keep Informed (low power, high interest), Monitor (low power, low interest)

---

**Q**: What is the difference between stakeholder engagement and stakeholder communication?
**Domain**: People | **Chapter**: 7
**A**: Engagement is two-way interaction and involvement in decisions; communication is one-way information sharing

---

**Q**: What should you do when stakeholder expectations conflict?
**Domain**: People | **Chapter**: 7b
**A**: Understand each perspective, identify common ground, facilitate discussion to align expectations, escalate to sponsor if needed for decision

---

**Q**: What are the key elements that make a group a "team"?
**Domain**: People | **Chapter**: 6a
**A**: Common goal (same outcome), interdependent roles (rely on each other), shared accountability (collective success/failure)

### Conflict Resolution and Decision-Making

**Q**: What are the five conflict resolution approaches?
**Domain**: People | **Chapter**: 6d, 8b
**A**: Collaborating, Competing, Compromising, Accommodating, Avoiding

---

**Q**: When is Collaborating the best conflict resolution approach?
**Domain**: People | **Chapter**: 6d, 8b
**A**: When both sides care, creativity is possible, relationship is important, and time allows for working together to find win-win solution

---

**Q**: When should you use Competing as a conflict resolution style?
**Domain**: People | **Chapter**: 6d, 8b
**A**: When you are confident you are right, speed is critical, or an important principle is at stake

---

**Q**: What is the difference between Compromising and Collaborating?
**Domain**: People | **Chapter**: 6d, 8b
**A**: Compromising means both sides give something up (half a loaf); Collaborating means finding a solution that meets both needs fully

---

**Q**: What are the six sources of power according to French and Raven?
**Domain**: People | **Chapter**: 6c
**A**: Legitimate (position), Expert (competence), Referent (relationship/respect), Reward (incentives), Coercive (penalties), Information (access to information)

---

**Q**: What is the most sustainable source of power for project managers?
**Domain**: People | **Chapter**: 6c
**A**: Expert power (competence) and Referent power (relationship/respect) because they build trust and influence without relying on formal authority

---

### Communication and Influence

**Q**: What are the key principles for building influence without authority?
**Domain**: People | **Chapter**: 6c
**A**: Understand the other person, build credibility, appeal to common ground, use data and facts, make it easy to say yes, know when to escalate

---

**Q**: What is the difference between intrinsic and extrinsic motivation?
**Domain**: People | **Chapter**: 6e
**A**: Intrinsic is driven by internal satisfaction (interesting work, pride); Extrinsic is driven by external rewards/punishments (bonuses, reprimands)

---

**Q**: What are the signs of team burnout?
**Domain**: People | **Chapter**: 6e
**A**: Exhaustion (physical/emotional), cynicism ("this won't work"), reduced effectiveness (mistakes, missed deadlines), withdrawal (going quiet)

---

**Q**: What are the four decision-making approaches?
**Domain**: People | **Chapter**: 6c
**A**: Autocratic (leader decides alone), Consultative (leader gets input then decides), Collaborative (team decides together), Delegated (someone else decides)

---

**Q**: When should you use Consultative decision-making?
**Domain**: People | **Chapter**: 6c
**A**: When the decision is important, different perspectives matter, and you want buy-in from the team

---

### Coaching and Development

**Q**: What is the GROW model for coaching conversations?
**Domain**: People | **Chapter**: 6f, 9c
**A**: Goal (what to achieve), Reality (current situation), Options (possibilities), Will (what they will do)

---

**Q**: What is the difference between coaching and mentoring?
**Domain**: People | **Chapter**: 6f, 9c
**A**: Coaching helps improve performance in current role; Mentoring helps develop for future growth and career advancement

---

**Q**: What are the core principles of servant leadership?
**Domain**: People | **Chapter**: 6f, 9b
**A**: Put team success first, develop people, listen deeply, build trust, empower decision-making, advocate for team, roll up sleeves

---

**Q**: How do you build psychological safety as a leader?
**Domain**: People | **Chapter**: 6d, 9b
**A**: Admit your mistakes, ask questions instead of asserting, respond well to bad news, protect people who dissent, follow through on promises

---

**Q**: What characterizes effective delegation?
**Domain**: People | **Chapter**: 6f
**A**: Choose right person, clarify the task and authority, set clear expectations and constraints, define success criteria, provide support as needed

---

**Q**: What are the characteristics of the four development levels in Situational Leadership?
**Domain**: People | **Chapter**: 9a
**A**: Enthusiastic Beginner (high motivation, low skill), Disillusioned Learner (building skills, dropping enthusiasm), Capable but Cautious (skilled, variable confidence), Self-Directed Expert (highly skilled, fully confident)

---

### Team Composition and Roles

**Q**: What makes an effective team composition?
**Domain**: People | **Chapter**: 6a
**A**: Functional expertise, cross-functional representation, subject matter expertise, organizational representatives, mix of seniority levels, diverse skills and perspectives

---

**Q**: What is RACI and what does each letter stand for?
**Domain**: People | **Chapter**: 6b
**A**: Responsible (does the work), Accountable (ultimately responsible for outcome), Consulted (input needed), Informed (needs to know decision)

---

**Q**: What are the key components of a clear role definition?
**Domain**: People | **Chapter**: 6b
**A**: Title/Name, Accountability (ultimate responsibility), Key Responsibilities (main activities)

---

**Q**: What accelerates team development through the stages?
**Domain**: People | **Chapter**: 6a
**A**: Clear goal and purpose, psychological safety, diversity of thought, good leadership, early wins, team continuity

---

**Q**: What decelerates or prevents team development?
**Domain**: People | **Chapter**: 6a

## Process Domain (41%)

### Project Initiation and Planning

**Q**: What are the key components of a project charter?
**Domain**: Process | **Chapter**: 10c
**A**: Purpose, objectives, success criteria, constraints, assumptions, authority levels, high-level scope, stakeholders, and project manager assignment

---

**Q**: What is the difference between constraints and assumptions in project planning?
**Domain**: Process | **Chapter**: 10b
**A**: Constraints are known limitations that restrict options (budget, timeline, resources). Assumptions are beliefs about uncertain factors that may prove false and become risks.

---

**Q**: What factors should influence the selection of a delivery approach (predictive vs. agile vs. hybrid)?
**Domain**: Process | **Chapter**: 10d
**A**: Requirements certainty, technology maturity, team experience, organizational culture, regulatory environment, stakeholder availability, project complexity

---

**Q**: What is the purpose of a business case?
**Domain**: Process | **Chapter**: 10a
**A**: Justifies the project investment by documenting the business need, analyzing options, estimating costs/benefits, and demonstrating alignment with organizational strategy

---

**Q**: What are the five Process Groups in project management?
**Domain**: Process | **Chapter**: 23
**A**: Initiating, Planning, Executing, Monitoring & Controlling, Closing

---

### Scope and Requirements Management

**Q**: What is the difference between scope validation and scope verification?
**Domain**: Process | **Chapter**: 11b
**A**: Scope validation is formal acceptance of deliverables by stakeholders. Scope verification is checking that deliverables meet requirements and quality standards.

---

**Q**: What is a Work Breakdown Structure (WBS)?
**Domain**: Process | **Chapter**: 11c
**A**: A hierarchical decomposition of project scope into smaller, manageable work packages that can be estimated, scheduled, and assigned

---

**Q**: What is the 100% rule for WBS?
**Domain**: Process | **Chapter**: 11c
**A**: The WBS must include 100% of the work defined by the project scope and capture all deliverables (internal, external, interim)

---

**Q**: What is scope creep and how do you prevent it?
**Domain**: Process | **Chapter**: 13c
**A**: Uncontrolled expansion of scope without adjustments to time, cost, or resources. Prevent through clear scope definition, change control processes, and stakeholder education.

---

### Schedule Management

**Q**: What is the critical path in project scheduling?
**Domain**: Process | **Chapter**: 11c
**A**: The longest sequence of activities through the project network; determines the minimum project duration and has zero total float

---

**Q**: What is the difference between free float and total float?
**Domain**: Process | **Chapter**: 11c
**A**: Free float is delay possible without affecting successor activities. Total float is delay possible without affecting project completion date.

---

**Q**: What are the two main schedule compression techniques?
**Domain**: Process | **Chapter**: 14b
**A**: Crashing (adding resources to critical path activities) and Fast-tracking (performing activities in parallel that were planned sequentially)

---

**Q**: What is the difference between leads and lags in scheduling?
**Domain**: Process | **Chapter**: 11c
**A**: Lead allows successor activity to start before predecessor finishes. Lag requires waiting time between predecessor completion and successor start.

---

### Cost Management and Earned Value

**Q**: What are the three main types of cost estimates?
**Domain**: Process | **Chapter**: 11d
**A**: Rough Order of Magnitude (-25% to +75%), Budget Estimate (-10% to +25%), Definitive Estimate (-5% to +10%)

---

**Q**: What is Earned Value Management (EVM)?
**Domain**: Process | **Chapter**: 14a
**A**: A methodology that integrates scope, schedule, and cost to measure project performance and progress objectively

---

**Q**: What do the EVM acronyms PV, EV, and AC stand for?
**Domain**: Process | **Chapter**: 14a
**A**: Planned Value (budgeted cost of scheduled work), Earned Value (budgeted cost of completed work), Actual Cost (actual cost of completed work)

---

**Q**: If CPI = 0.85, what does this indicate?
**Domain**: Process | **Chapter**: 14a, 14b
**A**: The project is over budget - for every dollar spent, only $0.85 of value is earned. Cost efficiency is 85% of planned.

---

**Q**: If SPI = 1.15, what does this indicate?
**Domain**: Process | **Chapter**: 14a, 14b
**A**: The project is ahead of schedule - work is being completed 15% faster than planned

---

**Q**: What is the formula for Cost Performance Index (CPI)?
**Domain**: Process | **Chapter**: 14a
**A**: CPI = EV / AC (Earned Value divided by Actual Cost)

---

**Q**: What is the formula for Schedule Performance Index (SPI)?
**Domain**: Process | **Chapter**: 14a
**A**: SPI = EV / PV (Earned Value divided by Planned Value)

---

**Q**: What is the most common formula for Estimate at Completion (EAC)?
**Domain**: Process | **Chapter**: 14b
**A**: EAC = BAC / CPI (Budget at Completion divided by Cost Performance Index)

---

**Q**: What does TCPI measure and what is its formula?
**Domain**: Process | **Chapter**: 14b
**A**: To-Complete Performance Index measures required cost efficiency for remaining work. TCPI = (BAC - EV) / (BAC - AC)

---

### Quality Management

**Q**: What is the difference between Quality Assurance and Quality Control?
**Domain**: Process | **Chapter**: 13b
**A**: Quality Assurance focuses on processes and prevention (doing things right). Quality Control focuses on deliverables and detection (checking if things are right).

---

**Q**: What are the three types of quality costs?
**Domain**: Process | **Chapter**: 11e
**A**: Prevention costs (training, planning), Appraisal costs (testing, inspections), Failure costs (rework, defects)

---

**Q**: What is the cost of quality principle?
**Domain**: Process | **Chapter**: 11e
**A**: It's more cost-effective to prevent defects than to find and fix them later. Prevention costs are lower than failure costs.

---

### Risk Management

**Q**: What are the four risk response strategies for threats?
**Domain**: Process | **Chapter**: 12c
**A**: Avoid (eliminate the risk), Mitigate (reduce probability or impact), Transfer (shift to third party), Accept (acknowledge and monitor)

---

**Q**: What are the four risk response strategies for opportunities?
**Domain**: Process | **Chapter**: 12c
**A**: Exploit (ensure opportunity occurs), Enhance (increase probability or impact), Share (allocate to third party), Accept (take advantage if it occurs)

---

**Q**: What is the difference between qualitative and quantitative risk analysis?
**Domain**: Process | **Chapter**: 12b
**A**: Qualitative uses probability/impact ratings and prioritization. Quantitative uses numerical analysis and modeling to calculate risk exposure.

---

**Q**: What is a risk register?
**Domain**: Process | **Chapter**: 12b
**A**: A document that records identified risks, their characteristics, potential responses, and assigned risk owners

---

**Q**: What is the difference between a risk and an issue?
**Domain**: Process | **Chapter**: 12d
**A**: A risk is a potential future event that may impact the project. An issue is a current problem that requires immediate attention and resolution.

---

### Change Management

**Q**: What is integrated change control?
**Domain**: Process | **Chapter**: 14c
**A**: The process of reviewing all change requests, approving changes, and managing changes to deliverables, organizational process assets, and project documents

---

**Q**: What is a Change Control Board (CCB)?
**Domain**: Process | **Chapter**: 14c
**A**: A formally chartered group responsible for reviewing, evaluating, approving, delaying, or rejecting changes to the project

---

**Q**: What is configuration management?
**Domain**: Process | **Chapter**: 14c
**A**: A collection of procedures to track project artifacts and ensure only authorized changes are incorporated into the project baseline

---

### Procurement Management

**Q**: What are the three main types of contracts?
**Domain**: Process | **Chapter**: 11f
**A**: Fixed Price (seller bears cost risk), Cost Reimbursable (buyer bears cost risk), Time & Materials (hybrid approach)

---

**Q**: When would you use a Fixed Price contract?
**Domain**: Process | **Chapter**: 11f
**A**: When scope is well-defined, requirements are stable, and you want to transfer cost risk to the seller

---

**Q**: When would you use a Cost Reimbursable contract?
**Domain**: Process | **Chapter**: 11f
**A**: When scope is uncertain, requirements may change, and you want flexibility but are willing to accept cost risk

---

### Process Groups and Integration

**Q**: Which Process Group typically requires the most time and resources?
**Domain**: Process | **Chapter**: 23
**A**: Executing Process Group, as this is where the actual work is performed to create project deliverables

---

**Q**: What is the key output of the Initiating Process Group?
**Domain**: Process | **Chapter**: 23a
**A**: Project Charter, which formally authorizes the project and gives the project manager authority to apply resources

---

**Q**: What is the purpose of the Monitoring and Controlling Process Group?
**Domain**: Process | **Chapter**: 23c
**A**: To track, review, and regulate project progress and performance; identify areas where changes are required; initiate corresponding changes

---

**Q**: What happens during the Closing Process Group?
**Domain**: Process | **Chapter**: 23d
**A**: Finalize all activities, complete administrative closure, conduct lessons learned, release resources, and transition deliverables to operations

---

**Q**: What is the relationship between Process Groups and project phases?
**Domain**: Process | **Chapter**: 23
**A**: Process Groups are not phases. All Process Groups can occur within each project phase, with varying levels of intensity

### Strategy and Portfolio Alignment

**Q**: What is the difference between a project, program, and portfolio?
**Domain**: Business Environment | **Chapter**: 15, 24
**A**: Project delivers specific outputs; Program coordinates related projects for strategic benefits; Portfolio manages all projects/programs to achieve strategic objectives

---

**Q**: What is portfolio management?
**Domain**: Business Environment | **Chapter**: 15b
**A**: Centralized management of projects and programs to achieve strategic objectives, optimize resource allocation, and maximize organizational value

---

**Q**: What are the key components of a business case?
**Domain**: Business Environment | **Chapter**: 10a, 15a
**A**: Business need, problem/opportunity statement, analysis of options, cost-benefit analysis, risk assessment, recommendation, and strategic alignment

---

**Q**: What is benefits realization management?
**Domain**: Business Environment | **Chapter**: 16a
**A**: The process of defining, planning, tracking, and ensuring that project benefits are actually achieved and sustained after project completion

---

**Q**: What is the difference between outputs, outcomes, and benefits?
**Domain**: Business Environment | **Chapter**: 16a
**A**: Outputs are deliverables produced; Outcomes are results from using outputs; Benefits are measurable improvements that create organizational value

---

### Organizational Change Management

**Q**: What is organizational change management?
**Domain**: Business Environment | **Chapter**: 18
**A**: The process of helping individuals and organizations transition from current state to desired future state to realize project benefits

---

**Q**: What are the stages in Kotter's 8-Step Change Model?
**Domain**: Business Environment | **Chapter**: 18b
**A**: Create urgency, form coalition, develop vision, communicate vision, empower action, create wins, sustain acceleration, institute change

---

**Q**: What does ADKAR stand for in change management?
**Domain**: Business Environment | **Chapter**: 18b
**A**: Awareness (of need), Desire (to change), Knowledge (how to change), Ability (to change), Reinforcement (to sustain change)

---

**Q**: What is change readiness assessment?
**Domain**: Business Environment | **Chapter**: 18a
**A**: Evaluation of organization's capacity and willingness to adopt changes, including culture, leadership support, resources, and past change experience

---

**Q**: What are common sources of resistance to change?
**Domain**: Business Environment | **Chapter**: 18d
**A**: Fear of unknown, loss of control, bad timing, lack of trust, poor communication, insufficient involvement, past negative experiences
