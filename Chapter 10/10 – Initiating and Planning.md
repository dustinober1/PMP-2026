# Chapter 10 – Initiating and Planning

## Introduction

Project Initiation
Organizational leaders authorize projects in response to organizational strategic-objective realization
and stakeholder needs. Projects enable organizations to make necessary changes to address these
factors. The factors can be categorized into several areas, including the following:
• Meeting regulatory, legal, or social requirements;
• Satisfying stakeholder requests or needs;
• Implementing or changing business or technological strategies; and
• Creating, improving, or fixing products, processes, organizations, or services.
By responding to these factors, leaders can enhance an organization’s viability. Projects provide the
means to make these changes and should ultimately link to the organization’s strategic objectives
and business value.

The way you start a project determines what becomes possible later. A well-initiated project has clarity about its purpose, realistic boundaries, appropriate authority, and an intentional delivery approach. A poorly initiated project stumbles through ambiguity, fighting unnecessary constraints, unclear about who decides what, and using a delivery approach chosen by default rather than design.

Initiation and planning are where project managers translate organizational strategy into actionable work. The [business case](../Chapter%2098/Appendix%20A%20–%20Key%20Formulas%20and%20Definitions.md#business-case) explains why the project exists. The [charter](../Chapter%2098/Appendix%20A%20–%20Key%20Formulas%20and%20Definitions.md#project-charter) authorizes the project manager to act. [Constraints](../Chapter%2098/Appendix%20A%20–%20Key%20Formulas%20and%20Definitions.md#constraint), [assumptions](../Chapter%2098/Appendix%20A%20–%20Key%20Formulas%20and%20Definitions.md#assumption), and [dependencies](../Chapter%2098/Appendix%20A%20–%20Key%20Formulas%20and%20Definitions.md#dependency) shape what's realistic. The delivery approach determines how the team will work. The roadmap communicates the journey ahead.

This chapter focuses on the critical early work that sets projects up for success. While initiation happens at the beginning, its effects echo through the entire project lifecycle. Strong initiation creates clarity. Weak initiation creates confusion, rework, and conflict.

## How You Start Determines What's Possible Later

Every project manager has inherited a badly initiated project. Perhaps there was no business case, just a directive from above. Perhaps the charter was vague about success criteria or authority levels. Perhaps constraints were never documented, leading to surprise limitations discovered mid-project. Perhaps the delivery approach was chosen by organizational default rather than project fit.

These initiation failures create predictable downstream problems:
- **No clear business case** means the project survives on momentum rather than value, making it vulnerable when priorities shift
- **Vague charters** lead to scope creep, authority conflicts, and unclear success criteria
- **Undocumented constraints** become surprise roadblocks that derail schedules and budgets
- **Wrong delivery approach** means the team fights their process instead of being enabled by it

Strong initiation doesn't guarantee project success, but weak initiation almost guarantees project struggle.

## Integration: How Initiation Differs Across Delivery Approaches

Initiation looks different depending on whether you're working predictively, adaptively, or using a hybrid approach. These differences aren't just about documentation volume; they reflect fundamentally different philosophies about how much must be known before work begins.

### Predictive Initiation: Comprehensive and Detailed

In predictive environments, initiation aims for comprehensive understanding before significant execution begins. The business case is detailed, analyzing multiple solution options with financial projections. The charter specifies scope, deliverables, milestones, and success criteria in detail. Constraints and assumptions are documented exhaustively. The delivery approach defaults to phase-gated progression with defined control points. The roadmap shows a detailed Gantt chart with dependencies mapped.

This comprehensive approach works when:
- Requirements are well-understood and stable
- Technology is proven and reliable
- Regulatory or contractual environments demand detailed planning
- Stakeholders need confidence through detailed documentation
- The cost of change is high, making upfront planning cost-effective

Sarah's office renovation project exemplifies predictive initiation. The business case analyzed build vs. lease options with 10-year cost projections. The charter specified square footage, occupancy dates, and regulatory compliance requirements. Constraints included a fixed move-in date and a capital budget cap. The delivery approach followed construction industry phase gates. The roadmap showed permitting, construction, and move-in milestones with critical path dependencies.

### Agile Initiation: Lightweight and Vision-Focused

In agile environments, initiation establishes vision and boundaries without attempting comprehensive planning. The business case articulates the problem and expected value, but solution details emerge through iterations. The charter defines purpose, success criteria, and team authority, but keeps scope flexible. Constraints focus on time and budget boundaries, with scope variable. The delivery approach uses iterative cycles with frequent stakeholder feedback. The roadmap shows release waves and capacity allocation, not detailed task schedules.

This lightweight approach works when:
- Requirements are uncertain or expected to evolve
- Technology is emerging or the team is learning
- Speed to market and learning are priorities
- Stakeholders can engage frequently for feedback
- The cost of change is low, making adaptive planning efficient

Sarah's expense system project exemplifies agile initiation. The business case identified expense processing pain points and expected efficiency gains, but didn't specify every feature. The charter empowered a small cross-functional team with authority to make design decisions. Constraints included a six-month timeline and budget for five team members, with features prioritized by value. The delivery approach used two-week sprints with stakeholder demos. The roadmap showed planned releases with expected feature themes, not locked-in scope.

### Hybrid Initiation: Balancing Fixed and Flexible

In hybrid environments, initiation balances predictive and agile elements. The business case is detailed at the program level but flexible at the delivery level. The charter specifies fixed elements (compliance, architecture, governance) while leaving delivery details flexible. Constraints include firm program milestones alongside adaptive team cycles. The delivery approach combines program-level phase gates with team-level iterations. The roadmap shows fixed program milestones with flexible delivery waves between them.

This balanced approach works when:
- Some requirements are certain (regulatory, architectural) while others are uncertain (features, UX)
- Large programs need coordinated delivery across multiple teams
- Stakeholders need predictability at the program level and flexibility at the delivery level
- Governance requires control points without micromanaging execution
- Different parts of the project have different risk and uncertainty profiles

Sarah's transformation program exemplifies hybrid initiation. The business case detailed compliance requirements and architectural standards (fixed) while leaving regional implementation flexible. The charter specified program governance, data migration milestones, and architectural principles, but empowered regional teams to adapt workflows. Constraints included regulatory deadlines (firm) and feature prioritization (flexible). The delivery approach used quarterly program gates with monthly regional releases. The roadmap showed compliance milestones and regional rollout waves with capacity-based planning for features.

## The Initiation Mindset: Clarity Over Certainty

A common initiation mistake is confusing clarity with certainty. Predictive initiation seeks certainty through comprehensive planning. Agile initiation accepts uncertainty and seeks clarity about vision and boundaries. Hybrid initiation distinguishes what needs certainty (regulatory, architectural) from what benefits from flexibility (features, workflows).

The project manager's role in initiation is to establish appropriate clarity for the delivery approach being used:
- In predictive projects: Ensure sufficient detail to enable confident execution
- In agile projects: Ensure sufficient vision to guide iterative discovery
- In hybrid projects: Distinguish what needs upfront detail from what needs adaptive learning

Poor initiation happens when these get mismatched. A predictive project launched with inadequate planning creates chaos. An agile project demanded to provide comprehensive upfront plans wastes effort. A hybrid project that treats everything as either fixed or flexible misses the balance.

Strong initiation creates the right clarity for the journey ahead.

This chapter is organized into five sub-sections, each addressing a critical initiation element:

Explores how projects get justified, including financial analysis, strategic alignment, and how business cases differ across predictive and agile contexts. Covers when and why business cases get revisited during execution.

Covers how to communicate the project timeline to stakeholders, from detailed Gantt charts to agile release plans to hybrid program roadmaps. Addresses different stakeholder communication needs.
