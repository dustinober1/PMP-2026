# Chapter 8 – Conflict, Negotiation, and Decision-Making

By month five of her projects, Sarah realizes that **conflict is not a sign of failure. It is a sign that people care.**

Two weeks ago, her architect and agile coach disagreed fundamentally on the expense system's technical approach. The architect wanted to build a scalable foundation; the agile coach wanted to build the simplest thing that works right now. Both were right. Neither was willing to back down.

Last week, the renovation project discovered that the proposed furniture layout would block a fire exit. The facilities manager wanted to redesign the layout (two weeks of delay). The general contractor wanted to use a narrower cabinet (potential safety issue). Someone had to decide, and the decision had consequences.

And in the transformation program, three regional directors proposed different implementations of the new process. Corporate insisted on standardization. The regional directors said standardization would not work in their contexts. Real conflict. Real stakes.

Sarah learns that conflict is not something to avoid or smooth over. **Conflict handled well yields better decisions.** Conflict mishandled destroys trust and derails projects.

This chapter focuses on the third pillar of the People Domain: how to **navigate conflict, negotiate differences, and make decisions** in ways that preserve relationships while achieving outcomes.

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

- Recognize sources of conflict and address root causes, not just symptoms
- Understand your own conflict style and when to use each approach
- Choose the right conflict resolution strategy based on the situation
- Negotiate scope, schedule, resources, and priorities in ways that create value for all parties
- Use collaborative problem-solving techniques to find solutions that satisfy multiple interests
- Make decisions decisively when consensus is not possible
- Escalate conflicts appropriately when they exceed your authority
- Recognize how different ways of working (predictive, agile, hybrid) require different decision-making approaches
- Lead through conflict and emerge with stronger relationships and better outcomes

Unlike Chapter 7, which was about *managing* stakeholders and *keeping them informed*, this chapter is about *engaging them in difficult conversations* where disagreement is real and the stakes are high.

## Integration: Sarah's Conflict Scenarios

Sarah encounters conflict in every project, in different forms:

**Office Renovation Team** (Predictive, ~30 people on contractor staff):
- **Scope conflict**: What is included in "move-in ready"? Furniture? Technology? Contingency spending?
- **Timeline conflict**: The contractor discovers asbestos; Sarah's priority is safety; Finance's priority is the March 15 deadline. Who decides?
- **Quality conflict**: The facilities manager wants high-end finishes; Finance wants cost control. This is a trade-off conflict.
- **Challenge**: How do you resolve conflicts when one party (contractor) is external and has contractual leverage?

**Expense System Team** (Agile, ~5 core team members):
- **Technical conflict**: Architect vs. agile coach on tech approach
- **Scope conflict**: "Must-have" features for launch vs. "nice-to-have" features the CFO (product owner) wants
- **Process conflict**: The CFO wants daily demos; developers say that is disruptive; QA says they cannot test that fast
- **Challenge**: How do you resolve conflicts when the project is moving fast and decisions need to be made daily?

**Transformation Program** (Hybrid, ~15-20 people across program):
- **Vision conflict**: Headquarters wants standardization; regions want flexibility
- **Approach conflict**: IT wants a big-bang implementation; operations wants gradual rollout
- **Stakeholder conflict**: Pilot teams feel their concerns are ignored; design team feels they are second-guessing decisions
- **Challenge**: How do you resolve conflicts across organizational boundaries where people have different power and incentives?

By the end of Chapter 8, Sarah understands that conflict is data. When two smart people disagree, both perspectives contain truth. Her job is to **surface the conflict, understand both views, and help the organization make a decision** that honors both perspectives—even if it means not getting everything anyone wanted.
