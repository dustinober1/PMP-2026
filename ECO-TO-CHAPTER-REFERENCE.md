# PMP Exam Content Outline (ECO) to Chapter Cross-Reference

This document provides a quick lookup table mapping all 26 PMP Examination Content Outline (ECO) tasks to the chapters in this study guide that cover them. Use this reference to find where specific topics are addressed and to understand how the guide provides comprehensive coverage of all exam content.

---

## How to Use This Reference

Each section below lists the ECO tasks for a specific domain. For each task, you'll find:
- **Task Title**: The official name of the task from the PMP ECO
- **Primary Chapters**: Chapters that extensively cover this task
- **Coverage Summary**: A brief description of how the chapters address the task requirements
- **Related Content**: Other chapters that provide supporting context

---

## People Domain (33% of Exam – ~58 Questions)

The People Domain focuses on leading teams, managing stakeholders, facilitating difficult conversations, and developing people. The 8 tasks in this domain emphasize servant leadership, psychological safety, and human-centered project management.

### Task 1: Develop and Communicate the Project Vision

**Primary Chapters:**
- **Chapter 7a (Identifying and Analyzing Stakeholders)** – Establishes who needs to understand the vision
- **Chapter 7c (Developing Communication Strategies)** – Explains how to craft and communicate vision to diverse audiences

**Coverage Summary:**
This task requires understanding organizational strategy, defining what success looks like, and communicating the vision clearly so stakeholders understand the project's purpose and value. The chapters show how to articulate vision in ways that resonate with different stakeholder groups and how to use the vision to guide stakeholder engagement throughout the project.

**Related Content:**
- Chapter 10a (Business Case and Project Justification) – The business case operationalizes the vision
- Chapter 3 (Value Delivery and Outcomes) – Foundational concepts of value and purpose

---

### Task 2: Manage Conflicts

**Primary Chapters:**
- **Chapter 8 (Conflict, Negotiation, and Decision-Making)** – Comprehensive coverage of conflict identification, resolution styles, and escalation

**Coverage Summary:**
This task requires recognizing conflict early, understanding different conflict resolution approaches (avoiding, competing, compromising, collaborating, accommodating), and choosing appropriate responses based on context. Chapter 8 shows how to facilitate productive conflict, when to escalate, and how to maintain psychological safety while addressing disagreements.

**Related Content:**
- Chapter 6d (Conflict Resolution and Problem-Solving) – Team-level conflict dynamics
- Chapter 8c (Negotiation Strategies) – Using negotiation to resolve conflicts

---

### Task 3: Lead the Project Team

**Primary Chapters:**
- **Chapter 6 (Building and Leading Teams)** – Covers team composition, development stages, roles, authority, influence, and servant leadership
- **Chapter 6b (Roles, Responsibilities, and Authority)** – Establishes clarity about decision-making and accountability
- **Chapter 6c (Influence, Power, and Decision-Making)** – Shows how to lead without direct authority

**Coverage Summary:**
This task requires understanding team dynamics (Tuckman's stages), building psychological safety, clarifying roles and responsibilities, establishing authority levels, and creating conditions where teams self-organize and perform. The chapters emphasize servant leadership, where your role is to remove impediments and develop people.

**Related Content:**
- Chapter 9 (Coaching, Mentoring, and Servant Leadership) – Developing individuals within the team
- Chapter 13 (Executing and Delivering Work) – Real-world application of team leadership during execution

---

### Task 4: Determine and Engage Stakeholders

**Primary Chapters:**
- **Chapter 7a (Identifying and Analyzing Stakeholders)** – Techniques for identifying all stakeholders and understanding their interests, influence, and impact

**Coverage Summary:**
This task requires discovering who is impacted by the project (both directly and indirectly), understanding their interests and power, mapping their engagement level, and determining how to work with them throughout the project. It emphasizes that stakeholders often extend beyond the obvious and that early identification prevents surprises.

**Related Content:**
- Chapter 7 (Stakeholder and Communication Mastery) – Overall stakeholder strategy
- Chapter 18 (Organizational Change and Readiness) – Understanding stakeholder readiness for change

---

### Task 5: Plan and Manage Stakeholder Engagement

**Primary Chapters:**
- **Chapter 7b (Planning Stakeholder Engagement)** – Creating engagement strategies tailored to different stakeholder groups
- **Chapter 7c (Developing Communication Strategies)** – Designing communication approaches that maintain engagement

**Coverage Summary:**
This task requires creating a stakeholder engagement plan that matches the engagement level and communication approach to each stakeholder group, adapting engagement as the project progresses and as stakeholder needs change. It emphasizes that engagement is ongoing, not a one-time activity.

**Related Content:**
- Chapter 5 (Core Project Management Concepts and Tools) – Foundational communication principles
- Chapter 14d (Stakeholder Management During Execution) – Managing stakeholders through project phases

---

### Task 6: Manage Communication

**Primary Chapters:**
- **Chapter 7c (Developing Communication Strategies)** – Designing communication plans appropriate to project context
- **Chapter 7d (Facilitating Difficult Conversations)** – Handling sensitive communication moments

**Coverage Summary:**
This task requires determining what information stakeholders need, when they need it, and in what format. It includes status reporting, escalation communication, crisis communication, and maintaining transparency. The chapters emphasize that communication isn't just broadcasting information—it's a dialogue that builds trust and enables decision-making.

**Related Content:**
- Chapter 5a (Stakeholders and Communication) – Communication fundamentals
- Chapter 14d (Stakeholder Management During Execution) – Communication during execution challenges

---

### Task 7: Help Ensure Knowledge Transfer

**Primary Chapters:**
- **Chapter 9c (Coaching and Developing Others)** – Guiding people toward their own solutions and building capability
- **Chapter 9d (Mentoring and Continuous Learning)** – Deliberately developing people and capturing organizational knowledge

**Coverage Summary:**
This task requires creating an environment where knowledge is actively shared (not hoarded), using coaching and mentoring to transfer knowledge through dialogue, documenting critical knowledge, and ensuring learning happens throughout the project—not just at the end. It emphasizes that knowledge transfer is a leadership activity, not just documentation.

**Related Content:**
- Chapter 9 (Coaching, Mentoring, and Servant Leadership) – Full treatment of knowledge transfer and development
- Chapter 14e (Project Closure and Transition) – Lessons learned and knowledge capture at project end

---

### Task 8: Support Organizational Change

**Primary Chapters:**
- **Chapter 18 (Organizational Change and Readiness)** – Change management strategies, resistance navigation, adoption, and sustainability
- **Chapter 18a (Change Impact Analysis and Readiness)** – Assessing who is impacted and whether the organization is ready
- **Chapter 18c (Communication and Training for Change)** – Building capability and commitment for change

**Coverage Summary:**
This task requires understanding that project success depends on successful organizational adoption of project deliverables. It requires assessing change readiness, designing change management strategies, building stakeholder commitment, and ensuring changes sustain after the project ends. It emphasizes that organizational change is as much a people challenge as a technical challenge.

**Related Content:**
- Chapter 12e (Change Management) – Scope change control and organizational change integration
- Chapter 13 (Executing and Delivering Work) – Managing teams during change

## Process Domain (41% of Exam – ~72 Questions)

The Process Domain focuses on structured execution of project work—from initiation through closure. The 11 tasks in this domain progress through the project lifecycle: plan the work, then do the work, monitor progress, control deviations, and close formally.

### Task 1: Develop an Integrated Project Management Plan and Plan Delivery

**Primary Chapters:**
- **Chapter 10 (Initiating and Planning)** – Project charter, business case, constraints, assumptions, delivery approach selection
- **Chapter 10a (Business Case and Project Justification)** – Creating compelling business cases that justify projects
- **Chapter 10c (Developing the Project Charter)** – Establishing project authority, scope boundaries, and success criteria
- **Chapter 10d (Selecting the Delivery Approach)** – Choosing predictive, agile, or hybrid based on project characteristics

**Coverage Summary:**
This foundational task requires creating a comprehensive project management plan that integrates scope, schedule, cost, quality, resources, procurement, risk, and communication strategies. It includes assessing project complexity, selecting the right delivery approach, and establishing governance. The chapters emphasize that the plan should match the project context, not follow templates blindly.

**Related Content:**
- Chapter 4 (Ways of Working and Tailoring) – Foundational concepts of delivery approaches
- Chapter 11 (Planning for Value Delivery) – Detailed planning across all knowledge areas

---

### Task 2: Develop and Manage Project Scope

**Primary Chapters:**
- **Chapter 11a (Requirements Collection and Refinement)** – Gathering, analyzing, and refining what stakeholders need
- **Chapter 11b (Scope Definition and Baseline)** – Defining what's in and what's out, getting stakeholder agreement

**Coverage Summary:**
This task requires clearly defining scope, creating a baseline against which changes are measured, and managing scope throughout execution. In predictive projects, scope is defined upfront and changes are formal. In agile projects, scope is discovered iteratively. The chapters show how to prevent scope creep while remaining responsive to legitimate changes.

**Related Content:**
- Chapter 13c (Managing Scope During Execution) – Scope management in real-world execution
- Chapter 11b (Scope Definition and Baseline) – Creating and maintaining scope baselines

---

### Task 3: Help Ensure Value-Based Delivery

**Primary Chapters:**
- **Chapter 13 (Executing and Delivering Work)** – Coordinating team execution to deliver promised value
- **Chapter 13a (Coordinating Team Execution)** – Different execution rhythms across delivery approaches
- **Chapter 13e (Team Performance and Accountability)** – Sustaining team performance to maintain value delivery

**Coverage Summary:**
This task requires ensuring that execution actually delivers the value promised in the business case. It requires understanding how different delivery approaches deliver value (predictive delivers at the end; agile delivers incrementally), coordinating teams to maintain quality, managing scope to protect value, and sustaining team motivation. It's the connection between planning and benefits realization.

**Related Content:**
- Chapter 11 (Planning for Value Delivery) – Planning for value from the start
- Chapter 16 (Benefits, Value, and Sustainability) – Ensuring value is actually realized post-project

---

### Task 4: Plan and Manage Resources

**Primary Chapters:**
- **Chapter 11f (Resource and Procurement Planning)** – Identifying resource needs, allocating people, and planning for skill development

**Coverage Summary:**
This task requires determining what resources (people, tools, facilities) are needed, securing them, allocating them across projects fairly, developing people to build capability, and managing resource conflicts. The chapters show how resource constraints shape schedules and budgets, and how to balance resource utilization with team development.

**Related Content:**
- Chapter 6 (Building and Leading Teams) – Team composition and development
- Chapter 13 (Executing and Delivering Work) – Managing team performance with allocated resources

---

### Task 5: Plan and Manage Procurement

**Primary Chapters:**
- **Chapter 11f (Resource and Procurement Planning)** – Determining what to buy, when to buy it, and from whom
- **Chapter 13d (Vendor and Procurement Management)** – Managing vendor performance and contracts during execution

**Coverage Summary:**
This task requires identifying what work will be performed by external vendors vs. internal teams, selecting vendors through fair competitive processes, negotiating contracts that protect both parties, and managing vendor performance throughout the project. The chapters emphasize that vendor management is relationship-based, not just transactional.

**Related Content:**
- Chapter 11f (Resource and Procurement Planning) – Procurement planning and strategy
- Chapter 13d (Vendor and Procurement Management) – Execution-phase vendor management

---

### Task 6: Plan and Optimize Quality

**Primary Chapters:**
- **Chapter 11e (Quality Planning and Acceptance Criteria)** – Defining quality standards and how quality will be achieved

**Coverage Summary:**
This task requires defining what quality means for the project (not perfection, but meeting stakeholder expectations), establishing quality standards and acceptance criteria, planning how quality will be assured, and integrating quality into the execution process. The chapters emphasize that quality is built in, not inspected in.

**Related Content:**
- Chapter 5c (Quality) – Quality concepts and tools
- Chapter 13b (Quality Assurance and Testing) – Quality execution during project work

---

### Task 7: Plan and Manage Schedule

**Primary Chapters:**
- **Chapter 11c (Schedule Planning and Work Breakdown)** – Breaking down work, sequencing activities, estimating durations, identifying critical path

**Coverage Summary:**
This task requires decomposing project work into manageable activities, sequencing them logically, estimating realistic durations, and creating a schedule that shows when work will be done and by whom. The chapters show how schedule planning differs across predictive (detailed upfront), agile (iterative), and hybrid (phased + iterative) approaches.

**Related Content:**
- Chapter 4 (Ways of Working and Tailoring) – How delivery approach affects scheduling
- Chapter 14b (Schedule and Cost Control) – Monitoring and controlling schedule during execution

---

### Task 8: Plan and Manage Finance

**Primary Chapters:**
- **Chapter 11d (Cost Estimation and Budgeting)** – Estimating project costs, creating budgets, planning contingency and management reserves

**Coverage Summary:**
This task requires developing realistic cost estimates using appropriate techniques (bottom-up, parametric, three-point, analogy), creating a budget that funds the plan, establishing contingency reserves for known risks and management reserves for unknown unknowns, and planning how costs will be controlled. The chapters show how to balance cost control with value delivery.

**Related Content:**
- Chapter 14b (Schedule and Cost Control) – Cost monitoring and control during execution
- Chapter 11d (Cost Estimation and Budgeting) – Budget planning and contingency management

---

### Task 9: Evaluate Project Status

**Primary Chapters:**
- **Chapter 14a (Tracking Progress and Performance)** – Selecting appropriate metrics, analyzing performance data, creating status reports
- **Chapter 14b (Schedule and Cost Control)** – Using earned value management and variance analysis to assess progress

**Coverage Summary:**
This task requires collecting accurate performance data, analyzing it to understand where the project stands relative to the plan, creating status reports that drive decision-making, and identifying when corrective action is needed. The chapters show how status monitoring differs across delivery approaches—formal reports in predictive, continuous dashboards in agile, tiered reporting in hybrid.

**Related Content:**
- Chapter 14 (Monitoring, Controlling, and Closing) – Full treatment of monitoring and control
- Chapter 12 (Risk, Issues, and Change Management) – Monitoring for emerging risks and issues

---

### Task 10: Manage Project Closure

**Primary Chapters:**
- **Chapter 14e (Project Closure and Transition)** – Administrative closure, contractual closure, lessons learned, benefits transition
- **Chapter 16 (Benefits, Value, and Sustainability)** – Ensuring benefits are realized and sustained post-project

**Coverage Summary:**
This task requires formal project closure including administrative activities, contractual closeout, lessons learned facilitation, knowledge transfer, and transitioning deliverables to operations. The chapters emphasize that closure is not optional and that how you close determines whether the organization learns from the project and whether benefits are actually realized.

**Related Content:**
- Chapter 16 (Benefits, Value, and Sustainability) – Benefits tracking and post-project realization
- Chapter 18 (Organizational Change and Readiness) – Ensuring changes sustain after project closure

---

### Task 11: Manage Project Artifacts

**Primary Chapters:**
- **Chapter 17 (Compliance, Risk, and Governance)** – Documentation, configuration management, audit trails, version control
- **Chapter 17c (Organizational Policies and Standards)** – Ensuring artifacts comply with organizational standards

**Coverage Summary:**
This task requires maintaining complete, organized records of project decisions, plans, changes, and outcomes. It includes version control, configuration management, and ensuring that artifacts are available for audits and future reference. The chapters emphasize that good documentation enables organizational learning and protects against disputes.

**Related Content:**
- Chapter 14 (Monitoring, Controlling, and Closing) – Documentation during execution and closure
- Chapter 17 (Compliance, Risk, and Governance) – Artifact management for governance and compliance

## Business Environment Domain (26% of Exam – ~45 Questions)

The Business Environment Domain focuses on strategic alignment, benefits realization, organizational change, compliance, and continuous improvement. The tasks emphasize that projects exist to create value within organizational, regulatory, and market contexts.

### Task 1: Plan and Manage Project Compliance

**Primary Chapters:**
- **Chapter 15 (Strategy and Portfolio Alignment)** – Ensuring portfolio decisions comply with organizational governance and strategy
- **Chapter 17 (Compliance, Risk, and Governance)** – Identifying and integrating regulatory, legal, and organizational compliance requirements

**Coverage Summary:**
This task requires identifying what compliance requirements apply to the project (regulatory, legal, organizational), integrating them into project planning from the start, designing processes that ensure ongoing compliance, and maintaining governance structures that verify compliance. The chapters show that compliance is not a separate activity but integral to project management.

**Related Content:**
- Chapter 10c (Developing the Project Charter) – Compliance constraints in the charter
- Chapter 14 (Monitoring, Controlling, and Closing) – Compliance monitoring during execution

---

### Task 2: Evaluate and Deliver Project Benefits and Value

**Primary Chapters:**
- **Chapter 16 (Benefits, Value, and Sustainability)** – Comprehensive coverage of benefits planning, measurement, and realization
- **Chapter 16a (Benefits Realization Planning and Identification)** – Defining benefits and establishing ownership
- **Chapter 16b (Tracking and Measuring Benefits During and After Execution)** – Designing metrics that measure outcomes
- **Chapter 16c (Long-Term Value vs. Short-Term Delivery Pressure)** – Making value-based trade-off decisions

**Coverage Summary:**
This task requires distinguishing between outputs (deliverables) and outcomes (benefits realized), developing comprehensive benefits realization plans, measuring benefits throughout and after the project, and ensuring benefits ownership extends beyond project closure. The chapters emphasize that project success is measured by benefits realized, not by on-time delivery.

**Related Content:**
- Chapter 15 (Strategy and Portfolio Alignment) – Strategic context for benefits
- Chapter 10a (Business Case and Project Justification) – Benefits promised in the business case

---

### Task 3: Evaluate and Address External Business Environment Changes

**Primary Chapters:**
- **Chapter 15 (Strategy and Portfolio Alignment)** – Understanding how market, competitive, and strategic changes affect portfolio decisions
- **Chapter 17 (Compliance, Risk, and Governance)** – Monitoring regulatory and legal environment changes
- **Chapter 18 (Organizational Change and Readiness)** – Understanding how external pressures drive organizational change needs

**Coverage Summary:**
This task requires monitoring the external business environment for changes that affect project viability or strategy, assessing impacts of those changes, and adapting project approach or scope accordingly. The chapters show that projects operate in dynamic environments and that successful project managers remain aware of external conditions.

**Related Content:**
- Chapter 12 (Risk, Issues, and Change Management) – Managing risks from environmental changes
- Chapter 14d (Stakeholder Management During Execution) – Communicating about external changes

---

### Task 4: Support Organizational Change

**Primary Chapters:**
- **Chapter 18 (Organizational Change and Readiness)** – Comprehensive coverage of change management, adoption, resistance navigation, and sustainability
- **Chapter 18a (Change Impact Analysis and Readiness)** – Assessing readiness and identifying impacted stakeholders
- **Chapter 18b (Change Management Strategies and Frameworks)** – Using structured approaches (ADKAR, Kotter) to manage change
- **Chapter 18d (Managing Adoption and Resistance)** – Handling resistance and building sponsorship

**Coverage Summary:**
This task requires recognizing that projects create organizational change, assessing the organization's readiness for that change, designing change management strategies that build adoption capability, communicating and training stakeholders, and ensuring changes sustain beyond project closure. The chapters emphasize that technical delivery doesn't equal organizational change—that requires explicit change management.

**Related Content:**
- Chapter 12e (Change Management) – Organizational change in scope change management
- Chapter 16 (Benefits, Value, and Sustainability) – Benefits realization through organizational adoption

---

### Task 5: Plan and Manage Risk

**Primary Chapters:**
- **Chapter 12a (Understanding Risk)** – Risk concepts, appetite, tolerance, and strategic importance
- **Chapter 12b (Risk Identification and Analysis)** – Identifying risks and analyzing probability/impact
- **Chapter 12c (Risk Response Strategies)** – Developing response strategies (avoid, mitigate, accept, exploit, enhance)

**Coverage Summary:**
This task requires proactively identifying potential problems (threats) and opportunities, analyzing their likelihood and impact, developing responses that either prevent problems or capitalize on opportunities, and monitoring risks throughout the project. The chapters emphasize that risk management is not risk avoidance—it's about managing uncertainty strategically.

**Related Content:**
- Chapter 11 (Planning for Value Delivery) – Integrating risk into planning
- Chapter 14 (Monitoring, Controlling, and Closing) – Monitoring risks during execution

---

### Task 6: Support Continuous Improvement

**Primary Chapters:**

- **Chapter 16d (Sustainability and ESG in Project Context)** – Integrating environmental, social, and governance considerations

**Coverage Summary:**
This task requires establishing practices that sustain project benefits and organizational improvements beyond project closure, learning from project experience to improve future performance, and building organizational capability continuously rather than in isolated projects. The chapters emphasize that continuous improvement requires intentional practices—it doesn't happen automatically.

**Related Content:**
- Chapter 14f (Project Closure and Transition) – Lessons learned capture
- Chapter 9 (Coaching, Mentoring, and Servant Leadership) – Building learning organizations

### Task 7: Understand and Consider Project Sustainability

This task requires integrating sustainability considerations (environmental impact, social responsibility, governance integrity) into project decision-making from selection through delivery. It includes understanding lifecycle costs, environmental footprint, social impact, and ensuring that project success doesn't create long-term organizational problems.

## Coverage Summary by Domain

All 8 People Domain tasks are comprehensively covered across Chapters 6-9 and integrated throughout execution chapters (13, 14, 18). The emphasis is on servant leadership, psychological safety, and developing people.

### Process Domain: Complete
All 11 Process Domain tasks are covered across Chapters 10-17. The progression follows the project lifecycle: initiate → plan → execute → monitor → control → close. Each chapter builds on previous knowledge.
