# 7.1d – Issue Management

## Learning Objectives

- Distinguish between risks and issues
- Identify when a risk becomes an issue
- Implement effective issue logging and tracking processes
- Prioritize issues for resolution
- Use escalation processes appropriately
- Resolve issues and close them
- Learn from issues to improve processes
- Understand how issue management differs across delivery approaches

---

## Risk vs. Issue: A Critical Distinction

The distinction between risk and issue is fundamental:

- **Risk**: Uncertainty that *might* happen. "There's a 30% chance the vendor will be late."
- **Issue**: A problem that *has* happened and requires immediate resolution. "The vendor just told us they'll be 2 weeks late."

Once a risk event occurs, it transforms from a risk into an issue. The risk of "vendor delay" becomes the issue of "vendor is late and we need to recover schedule."

### The Risk-to-Issue Transformation

This transition happens when:
1. **The risk event occurs** (vendor delay begins)
2. **The impact becomes real** (projects can't proceed without the vendor's delivery)
3. **Immediate action is required** (we can't wait for next month's planning review; we need to decide how to respond now)

The transformation from risk to issue changes your management approach:

| Aspect | Risk | Issue |
|--------|------|-------|
| **Timing** | Future uncertainty | Present problem |
| **Management** | Proactive prevention/mitigation | Reactive resolution |
| **Decision-making** | Strategic ("how do we prevent this?") | Tactical ("how do we solve this now?") |
| **Urgency** | Varies; can be monitored | High; requires quick resolution |
| **Accountability** | Risk owner monitors | Issue owner drives resolution |
| **Documentation** | Risk register | Issue log |

**Example from Sarah's office renovation**:

*Risk*: "City permits might be delayed. Probability: 40%. Impact: 2-4 week schedule slip."
- **Risk owner**: Facilities Manager
- **Risk strategy**: Monitor permit office; engage early; maintain schedule buffer
- **Management approach**: Monthly reviews; proactive follow-up

*When risk becomes issue*: "The permit office just informed us there's a 6-week backlog. Our permits won't be approved for 8 weeks."
- **Issue owner**: Facilities Manager (escalates to Project Manager)
- **Management approach**: Immediate decision-making. Options:
  - Expedite by applying pressure or paying expedite fees
  - Resequence the schedule; start other work first
  - Consider partial permits to begin some work early
  - Extend the overall timeline
- **Urgency**: High. Decision needed within days.

## The Issue Management Process

### 1. Issue Identification and Logging

Issues emerge from multiple sources:

- **Risk realization**: A risk we identified and were managing becomes real
- **Unexpected problems**: Issues we didn't anticipate (despite good risk planning)
- **Scope changes**: Stakeholders want something we didn't plan for
- **External events**: Regulatory changes, vendor failures, market shifts
- **Team feedback**: People doing the work identify problems blocking progress

**How to identify and log issues**:

1. **Create multiple channels for issue reporting**:
   - Daily standups (especially in agile)
   - Team meetings and retrospectives
   - Direct communication with project manager
   - Formal escalation channels (if risk doesn't get addressed)

2. **Log issues with enough detail**:
   - **What is the issue?** Clear description of the problem
   - **Why is it a problem?** What does it threaten? (schedule, quality, budget, stakeholder satisfaction)
   - **When was it discovered?** When did we first notice it?
   - **Who identified it?** Who reported the issue?
   - **Proposed resolution options**: What are possible ways to solve it?

3. **Create an issue log** (predictive) or **issue tracking board** (agile):

| Issue ID | Description | Priority | Owner | Status | Target Resolution |
|----------|-------------|----------|-------|--------|---|
| I001 | Users report slow system response in reports module | High | Tech Lead | Open | 3 days |
| I002 | Stakeholder wants additional reporting feature | Medium | PM | Investigating | Decision by next review |
| I003 | Team member will be unavailable week of 5/15 | Medium | PM | Logged | Cover plan by 5/10 |

**Example from Sarah's expense system**:

During sprint execution, the QA team discovers that the expense approval workflow doesn't handle certain edge cases correctly (users can approve their own expenses, which violates policy). This wasn't a risk that was explicitly identified, but it's now an issue requiring immediate attention.

- **Issue**: "Approval workflow allows self-approval"
- **Impact**: High (violates policy; unacceptable for launch)
- **Owner**: Tech lead (with QA team input)
- **Status**: Open
- **Resolution**: Fix code; add validation; increase test coverage
- **Timeline**: Must be resolved before sprint end (3 days)

### 2. Issue Prioritization

Not all issues are equal. Prioritize based on:

**Impact**: How much does this threaten the project?
- **High**: Blocks critical path; threatens launch; affects many users
- **Medium**: Manageable impact; delays some work; affects some users
- **Low**: Minimal impact; nice to fix but not critical

**Urgency**: How quickly must we resolve it?
- **High**: Blocking current work; requires immediate decision
- **Medium**: Needs resolution this week; can wait a few days
- **Low**: Can wait until next planning cycle

**Priority Matrix**:

|  | **High Impact** | **Medium Impact** | **Low Impact** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **High Urgency** | Resolve immediately | Escalate for decision | Expedite if possible |
| **Medium Urgency** | Resolve this week | Schedule resolution | Add to backlog |
| **Low Urgency** | Plan resolution | Accumulate and batch | Defer or accept |

**Example from Sarah's transformation program**:

| Issue | Impact | Urgency | Priority | Response |
|-------|--------|---------|----------|----------|
| Executive disagreement on program scope | High | High | Critical | Immediate escalation to steering committee |
| Insufficient training resources allocated | High | Medium | High | Resource request submitted; address this week |
| Team morale concerns from unclear role expectations | Medium | High | High | Clarify roles and communicate expectations immediately |
| Outdated documentation | Low | Low | Low | Add to backlog; address when capacity allows |

### 3. Issue Resolution

Resolution approaches depend on the issue's nature and the organizational context:

**For technical issues**:
- Debug and fix the root cause
- Test the fix thoroughly
- Deploy or integrate the solution
- Verify resolution

**For scope/requirement issues**:
- Understand what the stakeholder wants
- Evaluate impact on schedule, budget, quality
- Make a decision (approve, defer, or decline)
- Communicate decision and rationale

**For people/organizational issues**:
- Understand the underlying concern
- Address the root cause (not just the symptom)
- Communicate changes clearly
- Monitor for recurrence

**For vendor/external issues**:
- Escalate formally if needed
- Negotiate alternatives
- Prepare contingency plans
- Document agreements

**Example from Sarah's office renovation**:

*Issue*: Construction discovers that the HVAC system is more complex than the original design assumed. This wasn't included in the schedule.

*Resolution process*:
1. **Understand the issue**: The contractor, HVAC specialist, and facilities manager meet to understand exactly what's different
2. **Evaluate options**:
   - Replace the HVAC system entirely (high cost, long lead time)
   - Repair/upgrade the current system to meet requirements (moderate cost, shorter timeline)
   - Modify the design to work with the current system (lowest cost, minimal schedule impact)
3. **Decision**: Evaluate options against project constraints. Option 2 is recommended.
4. **Communicate**: Explain to the project team and stakeholders what changed and why
5. **Execute**: Contractor begins HVAC work; monitor progress
6. **Verify**: HVAC system is tested and meets design requirements
7. **Close the issue**: Document what was done and lessons learned

### 4. Issue Escalation

Some issues can't be resolved at the team level. They need escalation to higher authority.

**When to escalate**:
- **Decision authority needed**: The issue requires a decision only management/sponsor can make
- **Crosses team boundaries**: The issue affects multiple teams or projects
- **Threatens project viability**: The issue threatens schedule, budget, or scope materially
- **Involves policy or governance**: Regulatory, compliance, or organizational policy issues
- **Resource constraints**: Resolving the issue requires resources beyond the team's control

**Escalation process**:

1. **Document the issue**: Clearly describe the problem, why it matters, and what decision/action is needed
2. **Present options**: "Here's what we could do and what each costs"
3. **Recommend a path**: "Based on the risks and impacts, we recommend Option X"
4. **Escalate to appropriate authority**: Who has the authority to decide? PM → Sponsor? Team lead → Program manager?
5. **Get a decision**: Within a reasonable timeframe, the escalation recipient makes a decision
6. **Communicate the decision**: Back to the team and affected stakeholders
7. **Implement and track**: Execute the decision; monitor for success

**Example from Sarah's expense system**:

*Issue*: During user testing, the organization learns that a key reporting feature is complex and would require significant additional development work to deliver correctly by the planned launch.

*Escalation*:
1. **Document**: "Detailed cost reporting is more complex than planned. We can deliver basic reporting on time or delay launch 3 weeks for complete reporting."
2. **Options**:
   - Deliver basic reporting on time; add cost reporting in Phase 2
   - Delay launch 3 weeks; deliver complete reporting
   - Hire additional developer; deliver on time with full features (increases cost)
3. **Recommendation**: "Option 1 balances speed-to-value with quality. Initial users get a working system; advanced reporting follows based on feedback."
4. **Escalate to**: Finance department VP (stakeholder) and Program Manager
5. **Decision**: "Launch on time with basic reporting; Phase 2 adds cost reporting based on actual user needs"
6. **Communicate**: Product team updates stakeholders; sprint backlog adjusted
7. **Track**: Monitor phase 2 user requests to validate assumption about cost reporting needs

### 5. Issue Closure and Documentation

Once resolved, formally close the issue:

1. **Verify resolution**: The fix is tested/validated; the underlying issue is truly resolved
2. **Communicate closure**: Inform the person who raised the issue and affected stakeholders
3. **Document lessons**: What caused this issue? How can we prevent similar issues?
4. **Update processes**: If the issue reveals a process gap, update planning or execution processes
5. **Close the log**: Mark the issue as closed in the tracking system

**Example from Sarah's office renovation**:

*Issue*: (HVAC complexity issue from above)

*Closure*:
1. **Verification**: HVAC system is installed, tested, and meets design requirements
2. **Communication**: Project team and stakeholders are notified. No schedule change was necessary (Option 2 stayed within the contingency buffer).
3. **Lessons learned**:
   - "Older buildings have more complex HVAC systems. Future projects should budget more time for HVAC reviews."
   - "Early specialist involvement (HVAC engineer in design phase) would have surfaced this sooner."
   - "The contingency buffer we included prevented this issue from derailing the schedule."
4. **Process improvement**: Future renovation projects will include an HVAC specialist in the design review
5. **Closure**: Issue marked as closed; details filed in project archive

## Issue Management Across Delivery Approaches

### Predictive
- **Tracking**: Formal issue log; issues reviewed at project status meetings
- **Escalation**: Clear escalation path; steering committee or change control board handles high-impact issues
- **Prevention**: Assumes detailed planning catches most issues; actual issues are exceptions
- **Frequency**: Issues are logged and resolved throughout execution; formal reviews at gates

*Example: Sarah's office renovation maintains a formal issue log. Monthly project reviews include a section on "Issues and Resolutions." Any issue affecting schedule or budget is escalated to the facilities director and project sponsor for decision-making.*

### Agile
- **Tracking**: Issues appear on the team's backlog or issue board; they're given story points and prioritized
- **Escalation**: Issues that block the team or affect other teams are escalated in the daily standup or sprint planning
- **Prevention**: Assumes issues will emerge; the short iteration cycle allows rapid response
- **Frequency**: Issues are identified and resolved within sprint cycles; retrospectives discuss what issues emerged and why

*Example: In the expense system's daily standup, the team discusses blockers (which are typically issues). If a blocker can't be resolved within a day, it's escalated to the product owner or tech lead for priority handling. If it requires cross-team coordination, it's escalated to the program level.*

### Hybrid
- **Program level**: Formal issue tracking for strategic issues (organizational readiness, benefits realization, cross-initiative impacts)
- **Team level**: Agile issue tracking for delivery issues; escalation to program when they cross thresholds

*Example: Sarah's transformation program maintains a formal issue log for program-level issues (executive alignment, resource conflicts, organizational resistance). Individual initiative teams track delivery issues on their backlog and escalate to the program if they threaten the program schedule or benefits.*

## Issue Management Challenges and Solutions

### Challenge 1: Too Many Issues; Not Enough Resolution

**Problem**: Issues pile up faster than they're resolved. The log grows; nothing gets closed.

**Root causes**:
- Issues aren't prioritized; everything feels urgent
- Team lacks authority to resolve; everything escalates
- Ownership is unclear; no one feels responsible
- Resources are insufficient

**Solutions**:
- **Prioritize ruthlessly**: Only high/medium priority issues demand immediate attention
- **Delegate authority**: Give teams and owners authority to resolve lower-priority issues without escalation
- **Assign clear owners**: Each issue needs a person accountable for resolution
- **Set resolution timeframes**: "This issue will be resolved by Friday or escalated"
- **Review and close regularly**: Weekly reviews to close resolved issues and reassess priorities

### Challenge 2: The Same Issues Repeat

**Problem**: You resolve an issue, but it emerges again in a different context.

**Root causes**:
- You fixed the symptom, not the root cause
- You didn't update processes to prevent recurrence
- You didn't communicate lessons learned
- The organization doesn't learn from experience

**Solutions**:
- **Root cause analysis**: When an issue is resolved, ask "Why did this happen? What caused it?" and address the cause
- **Process improvement**: If the root cause is a process gap, update the process
- **Share learning**: Document lessons and share with other teams/projects
- **Retrospectives**: Review patterns of issues; look for common themes

### Challenge 3: Issues Block Progress

**Problem**: A blocker issue prevents the team from advancing. It's escalated but not resolved quickly.

**Root causes**:
- Decision-maker is unavailable
- The issue requires input from multiple parties
- There's no clear escalation path
- The issue is complex; no obvious solution

**Solutions**:
- **Escalation authority**: Make clear who has authority to decide and when they're available
- **Time-bound decisions**: "We'll make a decision by 2 PM tomorrow, even if we don't have perfect information"
- **Contingency planning**: "If we can't resolve this, here's our workaround plan"
- **Cross-team coordination**: Involve all affected parties in resolution; don't resolve issues in isolation

## Learning from Issues: Building Organizational Intelligence

The real value of issue management isn't just resolving problems—it's learning from them to prevent future issues.

**How to extract learning from issues**:

1. **Categorize issues**: What types of issues occur most often?
   - Technical issues (coding, integration)
   - Requirement issues (misunderstandings, scope creep)
   - People issues (coordination, skill gaps)
   - Organizational issues (misalignment, resistance)

2. **Identify patterns**: Which areas generate the most issues?
   - If integration always causes issues, invest in architecture or testing
   - If requirements keep changing, invest in better requirement elicitation
   - If certain vendors always cause problems, change vendors or contracts

3. **Root cause analysis**: For significant issues, do deeper analysis
   - "Why did this happen?"
   - "Why did that cause happen?" (keep asking until you reach the root)
   - "How could we have prevented this?"

4. **Share learning**: Document lessons and make them available
   - Issue archives for future reference
   - Retrospectives and lessons learned sessions
   - Process improvements incorporated into planning or execution processes
   - Training for teams on common issues

**Example from Sarah's projects**:

After the first few projects, Sarah's organization notices:
- **Pattern**: Requirements change mid-project across all projects
- **Root cause**: Insufficient stakeholder engagement during planning; assumptions about "what users want" aren't validated
- **Response**: Improve requirement elicitation process; include user representatives in planning; prototype early features for feedback
- **Result**: New projects have fewer mid-project requirement surprises

## Common Agile Pain Points and Troubleshooting

The *Agile Practice Guide* identifies specific "pain points" that teams often encounter when adopting or using agile. Recognizing these symptoms and knowing the standard agile "fix" is a key exam skill.

| Pain Point (Symptom) | Troubleshooting Possibility (Agile Fix) |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Unclear purpose or mission** | **Agile Chartering:** Build a charter with vision, mission, and success criteria. |
| **Unclear requirements** | **Collaborative Backlog Refinement:** Bring the team and Product Owner together; use **Personas** or **User Story Mapping**. |
| **Poor user experience** | **User Involvement:** Include UX design in the team; involve users early and often (demos). |
| **Inaccurate estimation** | **Relative Estimation:** Switch to Story Points; use **Spikes** to reduce uncertainty before estimating. |
| **Unclear work assignments** | **Kanban Board / Standups:** Visualize the work; team self-selects tasks (pull system) rather than being assigned. |
| **Team struggles with obstacles** | **Servant Leadership:** Scrum Master clears impediments; escalate if beyond team control. |
| **Work delays / Overruns** | **Slicing Stories:** Break work into smaller, vertical slices; focus on **Definition of Ready**. |
| **Defects / Poor Quality** | **Technical Practices:** Implement TDD, ATDD, Continuous Integration, and Pair Programming. |
| **Technical Debt** | **Refactoring:** Allocate capacity for code quality; make debt visible on the backlog. |
| **Too much complexity** | **Simplicity:** Focus on "the art of maximizing work not done"; MVP thinking. |
| **Siloed teams / Handoffs** | **Cross-Functional Teams:** Create teams with all necessary skills (T-shaped people) to deliver value end-to-end. |

## Key Takeaways

- **Issues are realized risks**: Once a risk event occurs and demands action, it becomes an issue
- **Issue management is reactive but can be systematic**: Formal logging, prioritization, and escalation processes turn chaos into manageable workflow
- **Prioritization drives focus**: Manage high-priority issues aggressively; defer low-priority; accept that not all issues will be resolved immediately
- **Clear ownership enables resolution**: Each issue needs a person accountable for driving resolution
- **Escalation prevents gridlock**: Clear escalation paths ensure timely decision-making
- **Learning prevents recurrence**: Root cause analysis and process improvement turn issue resolution into organizational learning
- **Different delivery approaches handle issues differently**: Predictive manages issues formally; agile embeds issue resolution in sprints; hybrid does both
