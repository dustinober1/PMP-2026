# 22d – Strategy Analysis

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this section, you will be able to:
- Analyze the current state of the business environment
- Define and describe the desired future state
- Assess risks associated with the change
- Develop effective change strategies
- Evaluate assumptions and constraints affecting the solution

---

## Introduction: From Current Reality to Future Vision

Strategy Analysis bridges the gap between where we are and where we want to be. It's about understanding the current business context, envisioning the future state, and determining the best path forward.

**Sarah's Strategy Analysis Journey:**
When Sarah began the office renovation project, she realized she couldn't just jump to solutions. She needed to understand:
- Why the current space wasn't working
- What success would look like
- What risks she needed to manage
- How to get from here to there effectively

Strategy Analysis provided that foundation.

## Current State Analysis

Understanding the current state is essential for identifying problems, opportunities, and constraints that will shape the solution.

### 1. Business Context Analysis

**Organizational Factors:**
- **Strategic Goals**: What is the organization trying to achieve?
- **Business Processes**: How does work get done today?
- **Organizational Structure**: How are teams organized?
- **Culture and Values**: What drives behavior and decisions?
- **Performance Metrics**: How is success measured?

**Environmental Factors:**
- **Market Conditions**: Competitive landscape, customer expectations
- **Regulatory Environment**: Laws, regulations, compliance requirements
- **Technology Trends**: Emerging technologies, technical debt
- **Economic Factors**: Budget constraints, resource availability

**Stakeholder Context:**
- **Key Stakeholders**: Who has influence and interest?
- **Stakeholder Needs**: What are their pain points and desires?
- **Relationship Dynamics**: How do stakeholders interact?
- **Communication Patterns**: How does information flow?

### 2. Gap Analysis

**Gap Analysis Framework:**
```
Current State → Desired State = Gap
```

**Types of Gaps:**
- **Performance Gap**: Where we are vs. where we should be
- **Process Gap**: Current processes vs. ideal processes
- **Technology Gap**: Existing technology vs. needed technology
- **Skills Gap**: Current capabilities vs. required capabilities
- **Resource Gap**: Available resources vs. needed resources

**Gap Analysis Process:**
1. **Document Current State**: Baseline measurements and observations
2. **Define Desired State**: Target conditions and capabilities
3. **Identify Gaps**: Compare current to desired
4. **Prioritize Gaps**: Focus on most critical differences
5. **Develop Solutions**: Address highest-priority gaps

### 3. Root Cause Analysis

**Purpose**: Go beyond symptoms to understand underlying problems

**Techniques:**

**The 5 Whys:**
- Simple but powerful questioning technique
- Keep asking "Why" until reaching root cause
- Example: Why are reports late? → Why is data entry slow? → Why is system poorly designed?

**Fishbone Diagram (Ishikawa):**
- Visual representation of cause-and-effect
- Categories: People, Process, Technology, Environment, Materials
- Helps identify multiple root causes

**Current State Analysis Tools:**
- **SWOT Analysis**: Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, Threats
- **Value Stream Mapping**: Visualize end-to-end processes
- **Benchmarking**: Compare to industry best practices
- **Surveys and Interviews**: Gather stakeholder perspectives
- **Process Mining**: Analyze actual process flows

### Sarah's Current State Example: Office Renovation

**Current State Findings:**
- **Physical Layout**: Cubicles create isolation, poor collaboration
- **Technology**: Limited meeting room technology, poor connectivity
- **Utilization**: 60% of space underutilized
- **Compliance**: Does not meet new safety regulations
- **Stakeholder Feedback**: 70% dissatisfied with workspace

**Identified Gaps:**
- Collaboration needs not met
- Technology infrastructure outdated
- Space not efficiently used
- Safety compliance issues
- Employee satisfaction low

## Future State Definition

The future state defines what success looks like. It must be clear, achievable, and aligned with organizational goals.

### 1. Vision Development

**Vision Characteristics:**
- **Inspiring**: Motivates stakeholders
- **Clear**: Easy to understand and communicate
- **Specific**: Concrete and detailed
- **Achievable**: Realistic and attainable
- **Aligned**: Supports organizational strategy

**Vision Components:**
- **Core Purpose**: Why this change matters
- **Future Picture**: What success looks like
- **Success Criteria**: How we'll know we've arrived
- **Guiding Principles**: Values that guide decisions

### 2. Future State Modeling

**Modeling Approaches:**
- **Process Models**: Future business processes
- **Organization Charts**: Future team structures
- **System Architecture**: Technical solutions
- **Physical Layouts**: Facilities and environments
- **Service Blueprints**: Customer experience design

### 3. Benefits Definition

**Types of Benefits:**
- **Financial**: Cost savings, revenue increase, ROI
- **Operational**: Efficiency, productivity, quality
- **Customer**: Satisfaction, loyalty, experience
- **Employee**: Engagement, satisfaction, retention
- **Strategic**: Market position, competitive advantage

**Benefits Realization Planning:**
1. **Identify Benefits**: What value will be created?
2. **Define Metrics**: How will we measure success?
3. **Set Targets**: What specific goals?
4. **Plan Measurement**: When and how to track?
5. **Assign Ownership**: Who is responsible?

### 4. Assumption and Constraint Analysis

**Assumptions:**
- Beliefs about the future without proof
- Must be documented and validated
- Pose risks if incorrect

**Assumption Categories:**
- **Business Assumptions**: Market conditions, customer behavior
- **Technical Assumptions**: Technology capabilities, constraints
- **Resource Assumptions**: Staff availability, budget
- **Schedule Assumptions**: Timeline feasibility
- **Regulatory Assumptions**: Compliance requirements

**Constraints:**
- Limitations on solutions
- Must be accommodated in design
- Shape solution options

**Constraint Types:**
- **Technical**: Technology limitations
- **Business**: Budget, timeline, policies
- **Regulatory**: Legal requirements
- **Environmental**: Physical, cultural factors

### Sarah's Future State Example

**Vision:**
"A collaborative, technology-enabled workspace that drives innovation and employee satisfaction while meeting safety standards and optimizing space utilization"

**Future State Elements:**
- **Physical Layout**: Open collaboration areas, quiet zones, flexible spaces
- **Technology**: Wireless throughout, smart meeting rooms, collaboration tools
- **Utilization**: 85% efficient use of space
- **Culture**: Increased collaboration and innovation
- **Compliance**: Meets all safety and accessibility standards

**Expected Benefits:**
- Financial: 20% reduction in real estate costs
- Operational: 30% improvement in cross-functional collaboration
- Employee: 85% satisfaction with workspace
- Strategic: Enhanced innovation capability

## Risk Assessment

Understanding and managing risks is critical for successful strategy execution.

### 1. Risk Identification

**Risk Categories:**
- **Business Risks**: Market changes, competitor actions
- **Operational Risks**: Process failures, resource shortages
- **Technical Risks**: Technology limitations, integration challenges
- **Financial Risks**: Budget overruns, ROI not achieved
- **Regulatory Risks**: Compliance failures, legal issues
- **Stakeholder Risks**: Resistance, lack of support

**Identification Techniques:**
- **Brainstorming**: Group discussion of potential risks
- **Checklists**: Standard risk categories
- **Expert Interviews**: Subject matter expert input
- **Historical Analysis**: Lessons from similar projects
- **SWOT Analysis**: Threats and weaknesses

### 2. Risk Analysis

**Qualitative Analysis:**
- **Probability**: How likely is the risk to occur?
- **Impact**: What is the effect if it occurs?
- **Urgency**: How quickly must we address it?
- **Risk Score**: Combined assessment (Probability × Impact)

**Risk Matrix:**
```
                Impact
                High  Medium  Low
            High    9      6      3
Probability Medium  6      4      2
            Low     3      2      1
```

**Quantitative Analysis:**
- **Expected Monetary Value (EMV)**: Probability × Impact
- **Monte Carlo Simulation**: Range of possible outcomes
- **Decision Tree Analysis**: Visual representation of risk decisions

### 3. Risk Response Planning

**Response Strategies:**
- **Avoid**: Eliminate risk cause or effect
- **Mitigate**: Reduce probability or impact
- **Transfer**: Shift risk to third party
- **Accept**: Acknowledge and prepare contingency

**Risk Response Planning Process:**
1. **Prioritize Risks**: Focus on highest scores
2. **Select Strategies**: Choose appropriate responses
3. **Develop Actions**: Specific mitigation activities
4. **Assign Owners**: Responsibility for execution
5. **Monitor Progress**: Track effectiveness

### Sarah's Risk Assessment Example

**Identified Risks:**
1. **Business Impact**: Renovation disrupts operations (High/High = 9)
2. **Technical**: New technology integration challenges (Medium/High = 6)
3. **Financial**: Budget overruns due to unforeseen issues (Medium/Medium = 4)
4. **Stakeholder**: Employee resistance to open office (Medium/Medium = 4)

**Response Plans:**
1. **Phased Implementation**: Minimize disruption through staggered rollout
2. **Technology Proof of Concept**: Test integration before full deployment
3. **Contingency Reserve**: 15% budget buffer for unforeseen issues
4. **Change Management**: Employee involvement and communication plan

## Change Strategy Development

Change Strategy determines how to move from current state to future state effectively.

### 1. Strategy Options

**Evolution (Incremental Change):**
- Gradual transformation over time
- Lower risk, longer timeline
- Preserves existing processes
- Good for stable environments

**Revolution (Transformational Change):**
- Rapid, comprehensive change
- Higher risk, faster results
- Breaks from existing patterns
- Good for urgent needs

**Hybrid (Strategic Approach):**
- Combination of evolution and revolution
- Balance risk and speed
- Tailored to specific needs
- Most common in practice

### 2. Strategy Selection Framework

**Selection Criteria:**
- **Urgency**: How quickly is change needed?
- **Risk Tolerance**: What level of risk is acceptable?
- **Resource Availability**: What resources can we commit?
- **Organizational Readiness**: Is the organization prepared for change?
- **Stakeholder Support**: Do we have necessary backing?

**Decision Matrix Example:**
```
Factor                    Evolution  Revolution  Hybrid
Urgency (Low-High)         Low        High         Medium
Risk (Low-High)           Low        High         Medium
Resources (Low-High)      Low        High         Medium
Readiness (Low-High)      High       Low          Medium
Support (Low-High)        Medium     Low          High
```

### 3. Implementation Approach

**Implementation Strategies:**
- **Big Bang**: All changes implemented simultaneously
- **Phased**: Gradual implementation in stages
- **Pilot**: Test with small group before full rollout
- **Parallel**: Old and new run together temporarily

**Factors Influencing Approach:**
- **Complexity**: How complex is the change?
- **Interdependencies**: How connected are systems?
- **Risk Level**: What is the impact of failure?
- **Resource Constraints**: What can we afford?

### 4. Value Stream Mapping

**Purpose**: Visualize how value flows through processes and identify improvements

**Process:**
1. **Identify Value Stream**: End-to-end process that delivers value
2. **Map Current State**: Document all steps, delays, handoffs
3. **Analyze Waste**: Identify non-value-added activities
4. **Design Future State**: Ideal process flow
5. **Develop Implementation Plan**: Path from current to future

**Types of Waste:**
- **Overproduction**: Making more than needed
- **Waiting**: Idle time between steps
- **Transportation**: Unnecessary movement
- **Over-processing**: Excess work beyond requirements
- **Inventory**: Excess work in progress
- **Motion**: Unnecessary employee movement
- **Defects**: Errors requiring rework

## Business Case Development

The business case justifies the investment in the change and provides the foundation for project approval.

### 1. Business Case Components

**Executive Summary:**
- Brief overview of the problem, solution, and benefits
- Key financial metrics (ROI, NPV, IRR)
- Recommendation

**Problem Statement:**
- Current state issues and impacts
- Business pain points
- Urgency and consequences of inaction

**Proposed Solution:**
- Description of recommended change
- How it addresses the problem
- Alternative options considered

**Benefits Analysis:**
- Quantified benefits where possible
- Qualitative benefits
- Benefits realization timeline

**Cost Analysis:**
- Development costs
- Implementation costs
- Ongoing operational costs
- Total cost of ownership

**Risk Assessment:**
- Key risks and mitigation strategies
- Risk-adjusted benefits
- Contingency plans

**Financial Metrics:**
- Return on Investment (ROI)
- Net Present Value (NPV)
- Internal Rate of Return (IRR)
- Payback Period

### 2. Financial Analysis

**ROI Calculation:**
```
ROI = (Financial Benefits - Total Costs) / Total Costs × 100%
```

**NPV Calculation:**
- Present value of future benefits minus present value of costs
- Considers time value of money
- Positive NPV indicates value creation

**Payback Period:**
- Time to recover initial investment
- Simple payback ignores time value of money
- Discounted payback considers time value

### 3. Business Case Validation

**Validation Process:**
1. **Peer Review**: Subject matter expert review
2. **Sponsor Approval**: Executive sign-off
3. **Financial Review**: Finance department validation
4. **Risk Assessment**: Independent risk evaluation

## Common Strategy Analysis Pitfalls

### Pitfall 1: Skipping Current State Analysis
**Problem**: Moving to solutions without understanding current reality
**Solution**: Thorough current state assessment before solution design

### Pitfall 2: Unrealistic Future State
**Problem**: Future state not achievable or aligned with constraints
**Solution**: Validate feasibility with stakeholders and technical experts

### Pitfall 3: Ignoring Stakeholder Perspectives
**Problem**: Strategy doesn't consider stakeholder needs and concerns
**Solution**: Comprehensive stakeholder engagement throughout analysis

### Pitfall 4: Overlooking Assumptions
**Problem**: Critical assumptions not identified or validated
**Solution**: Systematic assumption identification and validation process

### Pitfall 5: Inadequate Risk Assessment
**Problem**: Risks not identified or properly evaluated
**Solution**: Comprehensive risk identification and analysis process

### Pitfall 6: Weak Business Case
**Problem**: Insufficient justification for investment
**Solution**: Detailed financial analysis with realistic assumptions

## Summary: Strategy Analysis Foundation

Strategy Analysis provides the critical foundation for successful project outcomes by ensuring we:
- Understand where we are (current state)
- Know where we're going (future state)
- Manage the risks along the way
- Choose the right path forward (change strategy)

**Key Takeaways:**

1. **Current State Matters**: Thorough analysis prevents solving wrong problems
2. **Future State Must Be Compelling**: Clear vision motivates stakeholders
3. **Risk Assessment is Essential**: Understanding risks enables mitigation
4. **Change Strategy Impacts Success**: Right approach increases probability of success
5. **Business Case Justifies Investment**: Clear value proposition enables approval

**Success Indicators:**
- Clear understanding of current state gaps
- Compelling and achievable future state vision
- Comprehensive risk assessment with mitigation plans
- Appropriate change strategy selected
- Strong business case with realistic financial analysis

**Remember**: Strategy Analysis is not a one-time activity—it evolves as understanding deepens and circumstances change. Regular review and adjustment ensure continued relevance and effectiveness.
